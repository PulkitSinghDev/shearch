The Rape of Lucrece

  1. To the Right Honorable, Henry Wriothesley, Earle of Southhampton, and Baron of Titchfield.
  2. The love I dedicate to your Lordship is without end: whereof this Pamphlet without beginning is but a superfluous Moity. The warrant I have of your Honorable disposition, not the worth of my untutored Lines makes it assured of acceptance. What I have done is yours, what I have to do is yours, being part in all I have, devoted yours. Were my worth greater, my duty would shew greater, mean time, as it is, it is bound to your Lordship; To whom I wish long life still lengthened with all happiness.
  3. Your Lordships in all duty.
  4. Lucius Tarquinius (for his excessive pride surnamed Superbus), after he had caused his own father-in-law Servius Tullius to be cruelly murd’red, and contrary to the Roman laws and customs, not requiring or staying for the people’s suffrages, had possessed himself of the kingdom, went, accompanied with his sons and other noblemen of Rome, to besiege Ardea; during which siege, the principal men of the army meeting one evening at the tent of Sextus Tarquinius, the King’s son, in their discourses after supper every one commended the virtues of his own wife; among whom Collatinus extolled the incomparable chastity of his wife Lucretia. In that pleasant humor they all posted to Rome, and intending by their secret and sudden arrival to make trial of that which every one had before avouched, only Collatinus finds his wife (though it were late in the night) spinning amongest her maids; the other ladies were all found dancing and reveling, or in several disports; whereupon the noblemen yielded Collatinus the victory, and his wife the fame. At that time Sextus Tarquinius being inflamed with Lucrece’ beauty, yet smothering his passions for the present, departed with the rest back to the camp; from whence he shortly after privily withdrew himself, and was (according to his estate) royally entertained and lodged by Lucrece at Collatium. The same night he treacherously stealeth into her chamber, violently ravish’d her, and early in the morning speedeth away. Lucrece, in this lamentable plight, hastily dispatcheth messengers, one to Rome for her father, another to the camp for Collatine. They came, the one accompanied with Junius Brutus, the other with Publius Valerius; and finding Lucrece attired in mourning habit, demanded the cause of her sorrow. She, first taking an oath of them for her revenge, revealed the actor, and whole manner of his dealing, and withal suddenly stabbed herself. Which done, with one consent they all vowed to root out the whole hated family of the Tarquins; and bearing the dead body to Rome, Brutus acquainted the people with the doer and manner of the vile deed; with a bitter invective against the tyranny of the King, wherewith the people were so moved, that with one consent and a general acclamation the Tarquins were all exiled, and the state government changed from kings to consuls.
  5. From the besieged Ardea all in post,
  6. Borne by the trustless wings of false desire,
  7. Lust-breathed Tarquin leaves the Roman host,
  8. And to Collatium bears the lightless fire,
  9. Which in pale embers hid, lurks to aspire,
  10. And girdle with embracing flames the waist
  11. Of Collatine’s fair love, Lucrece the chaste.
  12. Happ’ly that name of “chaste” unhapp’ly set
  13. This bateless edge on his keen appetite;
  14. When Collatine unwisely did not let
  15. To praise the clear unmatched red and white
  16. Which triumph’d in that sky of his delight;
  17. Where mortal stars as bright as heaven’s beauties,
  18. With pure aspects did him peculiar duties.
  19. For he the night before, in Tarquin’s tent,
  20. Unlock’d the treasure of his happy state;
  21. What priceless wealth the heavens had him lent
  22. In the possession of his beauteous mate;
  23. Reck’ning his fortune at such high proud rate
  24. That kings might be espoused to more fame,
  25. But king nor peer to such a peerless dame.
  26. O happiness enjoy’d but of a few,
  27. And if possess’d, as soon decay’d and done
  28. As is the morning’s silver melting dew
  29. Against the golden splendor of the sun!
  30. An expir’d date, cancell’d ere well begun.
  31. Honor and beauty, in the owner’s arms,
  32. Are weakly fortress’d from a world of harms.
  33. Beauty itself doth of itself persuade
  34. The eyes of men without an orator;
  35. What needeth then apology be made
  36. To set forth that which is so singular?
  37. Or why is Collatine the publisher
  38. Of that rich jewel he should keep unknown
  39. From thievish ears because it is his own?
  40. Perchance his boast of Lucrece’ sov’reignty
  41. Suggested this proud issue of a king;
  42. For by our ears our hearts oft tainted be;
  43. Perchance that envy of so rich a thing,
  44. Braving compare, disdainfully did sting
  45. His high-pitch’d thoughts, that meaner men should vaunt
  46. That golden hap which their superiors want.
  47. But some untimely thought did instigate
  48. His all too timeless speed, if none of those.
  49. His honor, his affairs, his friends, his state,
  50. Neglected all, with swift intent he goes
  51. To quench the coal which in his liver glows.
  52. O rash false heat, wrapp’d in repentant cold,
  53. Thy hasty spring still blasts and ne’er grows old!
  54. When at Collatium this false lord arrived,
  55. Well was he welcom’d by the Roman dame,
  56. Within whose face beauty and virtue strived
  57. Which of them both should underprop her fame.
  58. When virtue bragg’d, beauty would blush for shame;
  59. When beauty boasted blushes, in despite
  60. Virtue would stain that o’er with silver white.
  61. But beauty, in that white entituled
  62. From Venus’ doves, doth challenge that fair field;
  63. Then virtue claims from beauty beauty’s red,
  64. Which virtue gave the golden age to gild
  65. Their silver cheeks, and call’d it then their shield,
  66. Teaching them thus to use it in the fight,
  67. When shame assail’d, the red should fence the white.
  68. This heraldry in Lucrece’ face was seen,
  69. Argued by beauty’s red and virtue’s white;
  70. Of either’s color was the other queen,
  71. Proving from world’s minority their right;
  72. Yet their ambition makes them still to fight,
  73. The sovereignty of either being so great
  74. That oft they interchange each other’s seat.
  75. This silent war of lilies and of roses,
  76. Which Tarquin view’d in her fair face’s field,
  77. In their pure ranks his traitor eye encloses,
  78. Where lest between them both it should be kill’d,
  79. The coward captive vanquished doth yield
  80. To those two armies that would let him go,
  81. Rather than triumph in so false a foe.
  82. Now thinks he that her husband’s shallow tongue,
  83. The niggard prodigal that prais’d her so,
  84. In that high task hath done her beauty wrong,
  85. Which far exceeds his barren skill to show.
  86. Therefore that praise which Collatine doth owe,
  87. Enchanted Tarquin answers with surmise,
  88. In silent wonder of still-gazing eyes.
  89. This earthly saint, adored by this devil,
  90. Little suspecteth the false worshipper:
  91. For unstain’d thoughts do seldom dream on evil;
  92. Birds never lim’d no secret bushes fear.
  93. So guiltless she securely gives good cheer
  94. And reverend welcome to her princely guest,
  95. Whose inward ill no outward harm express’d.
  96. For that he color’d with his high estate,
  97. Hiding base sin in pleats of majesty;
  98. That nothing in him seem’d inordinate,
  99. Save sometime too much wonder of his eye,
  100. Which having all, all could not satisfy;
  101. But poorly rich, so wanteth in his store,
  102. That cloy’d with much, he pineth still for more.
  103. But she that never cop’d with stranger eyes,
  104. Could pick no meaning from their parling looks,
  105. Nor read the subtle shining secrecies
  106. Writ in the glassy margents of such books.
  107. She touch’d no unknown baits, nor fear’d no hooks,
  108. Nor could she moralize his wanton sight,
  109. More than his eyes were open’d to the light.
  110. He stories to her ears her husband’s fame,
  111. Won in the fields of fruitful Italy;
  112. And decks with praises Collatine’s high name,
  113. Made glorious by his manly chivalry,
  114. With bruised arms and wreaths of victory.
  115. Her joy with heav’d-up hand she doth express,
  116. And wordless so greets heaven for his success.
  117. Far from the purpose of his coming thither,
  118. He makes excuses for his being there.
  119. No cloudy show of stormy blust’ring weather
  120. Doth yet in his fair welkin once appear,
  121. Till sable Night, mother of dread and fear,
  122. Upon the world dim darkness doth display,
  123. And in her vaulty prison stows the day.
  124. For then is Tarquin brought unto his bed,
  125. Intending weariness with heavy sprite;
  126. For after supper long he questioned
  127. With modest Lucrece, and wore out the night.
  128. Now leaden slumber with live’s strength doth fight,
  129. And every one to rest himself betakes,
  130. Save thieves, and cares, and troubled minds that forwakes.
  131. As one of which doth Tarquin lie revolving
  132. The sundry dangers of his will’s obtaining;
  133. Yet ever to obtain his will resolving,
  134. Though weak-built hopes persuade him to abstaining:
  135. Despair to gain doth traffic oft for gaining,
  136. And when great treasure is the meed proposed,
  137. Though death be adjunct, there’s no death supposed.
  138. Those that much covet are with gain so fond,
  139. That what they have not, that which they possess,
  140. They scatter and unloose it from their bond,
  141. And so by hoping more they have but less,
  142. Or gaining more, the profit of excess
  143. Is but to surfeit, and such griefs sustain
  144. That they prove bankrupt in this poor rich gain.
  145. The aim of all is but to nurse the life
  146. With honor, wealth, and ease, in waning age;
  147. And in this aim there is such thwarting strife
  148. That one for all, or all for one, we gage:
  149. As life for honor in fell battle’s rage,
  150. Honor for wealth, and oft that wealth doth cost
  151. The death of all, and all together lost.
  152. So that in vent’ring ill we leave to be
  153. The things we are, for that which we expect;
  154. And this ambitious foul infirmity,
  155. In having much, torments us with defect
  156. Of that we have: so then we do neglect
  157. The thing we have, and all for want of wit,
  158. Make something nothing by augmenting it.
  159. Such hazard now must doting Tarquin make,
  160. Pawning his honor to obtain his lust,
  161. And for himself himself he must forsake.
  162. Then where is truth, if there be no self-trust?
  163. When shall he think to find a stranger just,
  164. When he himself himself confounds, betrays
  165. To sland’rous tongues and wretched hateful days?
  166. Now stole upon the time the dead of night,
  167. When heavy sleep had clos’d up mortal eyes;
  168. No comfortable star did lend his light,
  169. No noise but owls’ and wolves’ death-boding cries;
  170. Now serves the season that they may surprise
  171. The silly lambs: pure thoughts are dead and still,
  172. While lust and murder wakes to stain and kill.
  173. And now this lustful lord leapt from his bed,
  174. Throwing his mantle rudely o’er his arm,
  175. Is madly toss’d between desire and dread;
  176. th’ one sweetly flatters, th’ other feareth harm,
  177. But honest fear, bewitch’d with lust’s foul charm,
  178. Doth too too oft betake him to retire,
  179. Beaten away by brain-sick rude desire.
  180. His falchion on a flint he softly smiteth,
  181. That from the cold stone sparks of fire do fly,
  182. Whereat a waxen torch forthwith he lighteth,
  183. Which must be lodestar to his lustful eye;
  184. And to the flame thus speaks advisedly:
  185. “As from this cold flint I enforc’d this fire,
  186. So Lucrece must I force to my desire.”
  187. Here pale with fear he doth premeditate
  188. The dangers of his loathsome enterprise;
  189. And in his inward mind he doth debate
  190. What following sorrow may on this arise.
  191. Then looking scornfully, he doth despise
  192. His naked armor of still-slaughtered lust,
  193. And justly thus controls his thoughts unjust:
  194. “Fair torch, burn out thy light, and lend it not
  195. To darken her whose light excelleth thine;
  196. And die, unhallowed thoughts, before you blot
  197. With your uncleanness that which is divine;
  198. Offer pure incense to so pure a shrine:
  199. Let fair humanity abhor the deed
  200. That spots and stains love’s modest snow-white weed.
  201. O shame to knighthood, and to shining arms!
  202. O foul dishonor to my household’s grave!
  203. O impious act including all foul harms!
  204. A martial man to be soft fancy’s slave!
  205. True valor still a true respect should have;
  206. Then my digression is so vile, so base,
  207. That it will live engraven in my face.
  208. Yea, though I die, the scandal will survive,
  209. And be an eye-sore in my golden coat;
  210. Some loathsome dash the herald will contrive,
  211. To cipher me how fondly I did dote;
  212. That my posterity, sham’d with the note,
  213. Shall curse my bones, and hold it for no sin
  214. To wish that I their father had not been.
  215. What win I if I gain the thing I seek?
  216. A dream, a breath, a froth of fleeting joy.
  217. Who buys a minute’s mirth to wail a week?
  218. Or sells eternity to get a toy?
  219. For one sweet grape who will the vine destroy?
  220. Or what fond beggar, but to touch the crown,
  221. Would with the sceptre straight be strucken down?
  222. If Collatinus dream of my intent,
  223. Will he not wake, and in a desp’rate rage
  224. Post hither, this vile purpose to prevent?
  225. This siege that hath engirt his marriage,
  226. This blur to youth, this sorrow to the sage,
  227. This dying virtue, this surviving shame,
  228. Whose crime will bear an ever-during blame.
  229. O, what excuse can my invention make
  230. When thou shalt charge me with so black a deed?
  231. Will not my tongue be mute, my frail joints shake?
  232. Mine eyes forgo their light, my false heart bleed?
  233. The guilt being great, the fear doth still exceed;
  234. And extreme fear can neither fight nor fly,
  235. But coward-like with trembling terror die.
  236. Had Collatinus kill’d my son or sire,
  237. Or lain in ambush to betray my life,
  238. Or were he not my dear friend, this desire
  239. Might have excuse to work upon his wife,
  240. As in revenge or quittal of such strife;
  241. But as he is my kinsman, my dear friend,
  242. The shame and fault finds no excuse nor end.
  243. Shameful it is: ay, if the fact be known;
  244. Hateful it is: there is no hate in loving;
  245. I’ll beg her love: but she is not her own;
  246. The worst is but denial and reproving.
  247. My will is strong, past reason’s weak removing:
  248. Who fears a sentence or an old man’s saw
  249. Shall by a painted cloth be kept in awe.”
  250. Thus graceless holds he disputation
  251. ’Tween frozen conscience and hot burning will,
  252. And with good thoughts makes dispensation,
  253. Urging the worser sense for vantage still;
  254. Which in a moment doth confound and kill
  255. All pure effects, and doth so far proceed
  256. That what is vile shows like a virtuous deed.
  257. Quoth he, “She took me kindly by the hand,
  258. And gaz’d for tidings in my eager eyes,
  259. Fearing some hard news from the warlike band
  260. Where her beloved Collatinus lies.
  261. O how her fear did make her color rise!
  262. First red as roses that on lawn we lay,
  263. Then white as lawn, the roses took away.
  264. And how her hand, in my hand being lock’d,
  265. Forc’d it to tremble with her loyal fear!
  266. Which struck her sad, and then it faster rock’d,
  267. Until her husband’s welfare she did hear;
  268. Whereat she smiled with so sweet a cheer
  269. That had Narcissus seen her as she stood,
  270. Self-love had never drown’d him in the flood.
  271. Why hunt I then for color or excuses?
  272. All orators are dumb when beauty pleadeth,
  273. Poor wretches have remorse in poor abuses,
  274. Love thrives not in the heart that shadows dreadeth,
  275. Affection is my captain, and he leadeth;
  276. And when his gaudy banner is display’d,
  277. The coward fights, and will not be dismay’d.
  278. Then childish fear avaunt, debating die!
  279. Respect and reason wait on wrinkled age!
  280. My heart shall never countermand mine eye.
  281. Sad pause and deep regard beseems the sage;
  282. My part is youth, and beats these from the stage.
  283. Desire my pilot is, beauty my prize,
  284. Then who fears sinking where such treasure lies?”
  285. As corn o’ergrown by weeds, so heedful fear
  286. Is almost chok’d by unresised lust.
  287. Away he steals with open list’ning ear,
  288. Full of foul hope, and full of fond mistrust;
  289. Both which, as servitors to the unjust,
  290. So cross him with their opposite persuasion,
  291. That now he vows a league, and now invasion.
  292. Within his thought her heavenly image sits,
  293. And in the self-same seat sits Collatine.
  294. That eye which looks on her confounds his wits;
  295. That eye which him beholds, as more divine,
  296. Unto a view so false will not incline,
  297. But with a pure appeal seeks to the heart,
  298. Which once corrupted takes the worser part;
  299. And therein heartens up his servile powers,
  300. Who flatt’red by their leader’s jocund show,
  301. Stuff up his lust, as minutes fill up hours;
  302. And as their captain, so their pride doth grow,
  303. Paying more slavish tribute than they owe.
  304. By reprobate desire thus madly led,
  305. The Roman lord marcheth to Lucrece’ bed.
  306. The locks between her chamber and his will,
  307. Each one by him enforc’d retires his ward;
  308. But as they open they all rate his ill,
  309. Which drives the creeping thief to some regard.
  310. The threshold grates the door to have him heard;
  311. Night-wand’ring weasels shriek to see him there;
  312. They fright him, yet he still pursues his fear.
  313. As each unwilling portal yields him way,
  314. Through little vents and crannies of the place
  315. The wind wars with his torch to make him stay,
  316. And blows the smoke of it into his face,
  317. Extinguishing his conduct in this case;
  318. But his hot heart, which fond desire doth scorch,
  319. Puffs forth another wind that fires the torch.
  320. And being lighted, by the light he spies
  321. Lucretia’s glove, wherein her needle sticks.
  322. He takes it from the rushes where it lies,
  323. And griping it, the needle his finger pricks,
  324. As who should say, “This glove to wanton tricks
  325. Is not inur’d; return again in haste,
  326. Thou seest our mistress’ ornaments are chaste.”
  327. But all these poor forbiddings could not stay him,
  328. He in the worst sense consters their denial:
  329. The doors, the wind, the glove that did delay him,
  330. He takes for accidental things of trial;
  331. Or as those bars which stop the hourly dial,
  332. Who with a ling’ring stay his course doth let,
  333. Till every minute pays the hour his debt.
  334. “So, so,” quoth he, “these lets attend the time,
  335. Like little frosts that sometime threat the spring,
  336. To add a more rejoicing to the prime,
  337. And give the sneaped birds more cause to sing.
  338. Pain pays the income of each precious thing:
  339. Huge rocks, high winds, strong pirates, shelves and sands
  340. The merchant fears, ere rich at home he lands.”
  341. Now is he come unto the chamber door
  342. That shuts him from the heaven of his thought,
  343. Which with a yielding latch, and with no more,
  344. Hath barr’d him from the blessed thing he sought.
  345. So from himself impiety hath wrought,
  346. That for his prey to pray he doth begin,
  347. As if the heavens should countenance his sin.
  348. But in the midst of his unfruitful prayer,
  349. Having solicited th’ eternal power
  350. That his foul thoughts might compass his fair fair,
  351. And they would stand auspicious to the hour,
  352. Even there he starts: quoth he, “I must deflow’r;
  353. The powers to whom I pray abhor this fact,
  354. How can they then assist me in the act?
  355. Then Love and Fortune be my gods, my guide!
  356. My will is back’d with resolution.
  357. Thoughts are but dreams till their effects be tried,
  358. The blackest sin is clear’d with absolution;
  359. Against love’s fire fear’s frost hath dissolution.
  360. The eye of heaven is out, and misty night
  361. Covers the shame that follows sweet delight.”
  362. This said, his guilty hand pluck’d up the latch,
  363. And with his knee the door he opens wide.
  364. The dove sleeps fast that this night-owl will catch;
  365. Thus treason works ere traitors be espied.
  366. Who sees the lurking serpent steps aside;
  367. But she sound sleeping, fearing no such thing,
  368. Lies at the mercy of his mortal sting.
  369. Into the chamber wickedly he stalks,
  370. And gazeth on her yet unstained bed.
  371. The curtains being close, about he walks,
  372. Rolling his greedy eyeballs in his head.
  373. By their high treason is his heart misled,
  374. Which gives the watch-word to his hand full soon
  375. To draw the cloud that hides the silver moon.
  376. Look as the fair and fiery-pointed sun,
  377. Rushing from forth a cloud, bereaves our sight,
  378. Even so the curtain drawn, his eyes begun
  379. To wink, being blinded with a greater light:
  380. Whether it is that she reflects so bright
  381. That dazzleth them, or else some shame supposed,
  382. But blind they are, and keep themselves enclosed.
  383. O had they in that darksome prison died,
  384. Then had they seen the period of their ill!
  385. Then Collatine again by Lucrece’ side
  386. In his clear bed might have reposed still.
  387. But they must ope, this blessed league to kill,
  388. And holy-thoughted Lucrece to their sight
  389. Must sell her joy, her life, her world’s delight.
  390. Her lily hand her rosy cheek lies under,
  391. Coz’ning the pillow of a lawful kiss;
  392. Who therefore angry seems to part in sunder,
  393. Swelling on either side to want his bliss;
  394. Between whose hills her head entombed is;
  395. Where like a virtuous monument she lies,
  396. To be admir’d of lewd unhallowed eyes.
  397. Without the bed her other fair hand was,
  398. On the green coverlet, whose perfect white
  399. Show’d like an April daisy on the grass,
  400. With pearly sweat resembling dew of night.
  401. Her eyes like marigolds had sheath’d their light,
  402. And canopied in darkness sweetly lay,
  403. Till they might open to adorn the day.
  404. Her hair like golden threads play’d with her breath—
  405. O modest wantons, wanton modesty!—
  406. Showing life’s triumph in the map of death,
  407. And death’s dim look in life’s mortality.
  408. Each in her sleep themselves so beautify,
  409. As if between them twain there were no strife,
  410. But that life liv’d in death, and death in life.
  411. Her breasts like ivory globes circled with blue,
  412. A pair of maiden worlds unconquered,
  413. Save of their lord no bearing yoke they knew,
  414. And him by oath they truly honored.
  415. These worlds in Tarquin new ambition bred,
  416. Who like a foul usurper went about
  417. From this fair throne to heave the owner out.
  418. What could he see but mightily he noted?
  419. What did he note but strongly he desired?
  420. What he beheld, on that he firmly doted,
  421. And in his will his willful eye he tired.
  422. With more than admiration he admired
  423. Her azure veins, her alabaster skin,
  424. Her coral lips, her snow-white dimpled chin.
  425. As the grim lion fawneth o’er his prey,
  426. Sharp hunger by the conquest satisfied,
  427. So o’er this sleeping soul doth Tarquin stay,
  428. His rage of lust by gazing qualified;
  429. Slak’d, not suppress’d, for standing by her side,
  430. His eye, which late this mutiny restrains,
  431. Unto a greater uproar tempts his veins.
  432. And they like straggling slaves for pillage fighting,
  433. Obdurate vassals fell exploits effecting,
  434. In bloody death and ravishment delighting,
  435. Nor children’s tears nor mothers’ groans respecting,
  436. Swell in their pride, the onset still expecting.
  437. Anon his beating heart, alarum striking,
  438. Gives the hot charge, and bids them do their liking.
  439. His drumming heart cheers up his burning eye,
  440. His eye commends the leading to his hand;
  441. His hand as proud of such a dignity,
  442. Smoking with pride, march’d on, to make his stand
  443. On her bare breast, the heart of all her land;
  444. Whose ranks of blue veins, as his hand did scale,
  445. Left their round turrets destitute and pale.
  446. They must’ring to the quiet cabinet
  447. Where their dear governess and lady lies,
  448. Do tell her she is dreadfully beset,
  449. And fright her with confusion of their cries.
  450. She much amaz’d breaks ope her lock’d-up eyes,
  451. Who peeping forth this tumult to behold,
  452. Are by his flaming torch dimm’d and controll’d.
  453. Imagine her as one in dead of night
  454. From forth dull sleep by dreadful fancy waking,
  455. That thinks she hath beheld some ghastly sprite,
  456. Whose grim aspect sets every joint a-shaking;
  457. What terror ’tis! But she in worser taking,
  458. From sleep disturbed, heedfully doth view
  459. The sight which makes supposed terror true.
  460. Wrapp’d and confounded in a thousand fears,
  461. Like to a new-kill’d bird she trembling lies;
  462. She dares not look, yet winking there appears
  463. Quick-shifting antics, ugly in her eyes.
  464. Such shadows are the weak brain’s forgeries,
  465. Who angry that the eyes fly from their lights,
  466. In darkness daunts them with more dreadful sights.
  467. His hand that yet remains upon her breast
  468. (Rude ram, to batter such an ivory wall!)
  469. May feel her heart (poor citizen!) distress’d,
  470. Wounding itself to death, rise up and fall,
  471. Beating her bulk, that his hand shakes withal.
  472. This moves in him more rage and lesser pity
  473. To make the breach and enter this sweet city.
  474. First like a trumpet doth his tongue begin
  475. To sound a parley to his heartless foe,
  476. Who o’er the white sheet peers her whiter chin,
  477. The reason of this rash alarm to know,
  478. Which he by dumb demeanor seeks to show;
  479. But she with vehement prayers urgeth still
  480. Under what color he commits this ill.
  481. Thus he replies: “The color in thy face,
  482. That even for anger makes the lily pale,
  483. And the red rose blush at her own disgrace,
  484. Shall plead for me and tell my loving tale.
  485. Under that color am I come to scale
  486. Thy never-conquered fort; the fault is thine,
  487. For those thine eyes betray thee unto mine.
  488. Thus I forestall thee, if thou mean to chide,
  489. Thy beauty hath ensnar’d thee to this night,
  490. Where thou with patience must my will abide—
  491. My will that marks thee for my earth’s delight,
  492. Which I to conquer sought with all my might;
  493. But as reproof and reason beat it dead,
  494. By thy bright beauty was it newly bred.
  495. I see what crosses my attempt will bring,
  496. I know what thorns the growing rose defends,
  497. I think the honey guarded with a sting:
  498. All this beforehand counsel comprehends.
  499. But Will is deaf and hears no heedful friends;
  500. Only he hath an eye to gaze on beauty,
  501. And dotes on what he looks, ’gainst law or duty.
  502. I have debated, even in my soul,
  503. What wrong, what shame, what sorrow I shall breed,
  504. But nothing can affection’s course control,
  505. Or stop the headlong fury of his speed.
  506. I know repentant tears ensue the deed,
  507. Reproach, disdain, and deadly enmity,
  508. Yet strive I to embrace mine infamy.”
  509. This said, he shakes aloft his Roman blade,
  510. Which like a falcon tow’ring in the skies,
  511. Coucheth the fowl below with his wings’ shade,
  512. Whose crooked beak threats, if he mount, he dies:
  513. So under his insulting falchion lies
  514. Harmless Lucretia, marking what he tells
  515. With trembling fear, as fowl hear falcons’ bells.
  516. “Lucrece,” quoth he, “this night I must enjoy thee
  517. If thou deny, then force must work my way,
  518. For in thy bed I purpose to destroy thee.
  519. That done, some worthless slave of thine I’ll slay,
  520. To kill thine honor with thy live’s decay;
  521. And in thy dead arms do I mean to place him,
  522. Swearing I slew him, seeing thee embrace him.
  523. So thy surviving husband shall remain
  524. The scornful mark of every open eye;
  525. Thy kinsmen hang their heads at this disdain,
  526. Thy issue blurr’d with nameless bastardy;
  527. And thou, the author of their obloquy,
  528. Shalt have thy trespass cited up in rhymes,
  529. And sung by children in succeeding times.
  530. But if thou yield, I rest thy secret friend:
  531. The fault unknown is as a thought unacted.
  532. A little harm done to a great good end
  533. For lawful policy remains enacted.
  534. The poisonous simple sometime is compacted
  535. In a pure compound; being so applied,
  536. His venom in effect is purified.
  537. Then for thy husband and thy children’s sake,
  538. Tender my suit; bequeath not to their lot
  539. The shame that from them no device can take,
  540. The blemish that will never be forgot,
  541. Worse than a slavish wipe, or birth-hour’s blot;
  542. For marks descried in men’s nativity
  543. Are nature’s faults, not their own infamy.”
  544. Here with a cockatrice’ dead-killing eye
  545. He rouseth up himself, and makes a pause;
  546. While she, the picture of pure piety,
  547. Like a white hind under the gripe’s sharp claws,
  548. Pleads in a wilderness where are no laws,
  549. To the rough beast that knows no gentle right,
  550. Nor aught obeys but his foul appetite.
  551. But when a black-fac’d cloud the world doth threat,
  552. In his dim mist th’ aspiring mountains hiding,
  553. From earth’s dark womb some gentle gust doth get,
  554. Which blow these pitchy vapors from their biding,
  555. Hind’ring their present fall by this dividing;
  556. So his unhallowed haste her words delays,
  557. And moody Pluto winks while Orpheus plays.
  558. Yet, foul night-waking cat, he doth but dally,
  559. While in his hold-fast foot the weak mouse panteth.
  560. Her sad behavior feeds his vulture folly,
  561. A swallowing gulf that even in plenty wanteth.
  562. His ear her prayers admits, but his heart granteth
  563. No penetrable entrance to her plaining:
  564. Tears harden lust, though marble wear with raining.
  565. Her pity-pleading eyes are sadly fixed
  566. In the remorseless wrinkles of his face;
  567. Her modest eloquence with sighs is mixed,
  568. Which to her oratory adds more grace.
  569. She puts the period often from his place,
  570. And midst the sentence so her accent breaks,
  571. That twice she doth begin ere once she speaks.
  572. She conjures him by high almighty Jove,
  573. By knighthood, gentry, and sweet friendship’s oath,
  574. By her untimely tears, her husband’s love,
  575. By holy human law, and common troth,
  576. By heaven and earth, and all the power of both,
  577. That to his borrowed bed he make retire,
  578. And stoop to honor, not to foul desire.
  579. Quoth she, “Reward not hospitality
  580. With such black payment as thou hast pretended;
  581. Mud not the fountain that gave drink to thee,
  582. Mar not the thing that cannot be amended.
  583. End thy ill aim before thy shoot be ended;
  584. He is no woodman that doth bend his bow
  585. To strike a poor unseasonable doe.
  586. My husband is thy friend, for his sake spare me;
  587. Thyself art mighty, for thine own sake leave me;
  588. Myself a weakling, do not then ensnare me;
  589. Thou look’st not like deceit, do not deceive me.
  590. My sighs like whirlwinds labor hence to heave thee.
  591. If ever man were mov’d with woman’s moans,
  592. Be moved with my tears, my sighs, my groans.
  593. All which together, like a troubled ocean,
  594. Beat at thy rocky and wrack-threat’ning heart,
  595. To soften it with their continual motion;
  596. For stones dissolv’d to water do convert.
  597. O, if no harder than a stone thou art,
  598. Melt at my tears and be compassionate!
  599. Soft pity enters at an iron gate.
  600. In Tarquin’s likeness I did entertain thee;
  601. Hast thou put on his shape to do him shame?
  602. To all the host of heaven I complain me:
  603. Thou wrong’st his honor, wound’st his princely name.
  604. Thou art not what thou seem’st, and if the same,
  605. Thou seem’st not what thou art, a god, a king;
  606. For kings like gods should govern every thing.
  607. How will thy shame be seeded in thine age
  608. When thus thy vices bud before thy spring?
  609. If in thy hope thou dar’st do such outrage,
  610. What dar’st thou not when once thou art a king?
  611. O, be rememb’red, no outrageous thing
  612. From vassal actors can be wip’d away;
  613. Then kings’ misdeeds cannot be hid in clay.
  614. This deed will make thee only lov’d for fear,
  615. But happy monarchs still are fear’d for love;
  616. With foul offenders thou perforce must bear,
  617. When they in thee the like offenses prove.
  618. If but for fear of this, thy will remove;
  619. For princes are the glass, the school, the book,
  620. Where subjects’ eyes do learn, do read, do look.
  621. And wilt thou be the school where Lust shall learn?
  622. Must he in thee read lectures of such shame?
  623. wilt thou be glass wherein it shall discern
  624. Authority for sin, warrant for blame,
  625. To privilege dishonor in thy name?
  626. Thou back’st reproach against long-living laud,
  627. And mak’st fair reputation but a bawd.
  628. Hast thou command? By him that gave it thee,
  629. From a pure heart command thy rebel will;
  630. Draw not thy sword to guard iniquity,
  631. For it was lent thee all that brood to kill.
  632. Thy princely office how canst thou fulfill,
  633. When pattern’d by thy fault foul Sin may say
  634. He learn’d to sin, and thou didst teach the way?
  635. Think but how vile a spectacle it were
  636. To view thy present trespass in another.
  637. Men’s faults do seldom to themselves appear,
  638. Their own transgressions partially they smother:
  639. This guilt would seem death-worthy in thy brother.
  640. O how are they wrapp’d in with infamies
  641. That from their own misdeeds askaunce their eyes!
  642. To thee, to thee, my heav’d-up hands appeal,
  643. Not to seducing lust, thy rash relier.
  644. I sue for exil’d majesty’s repeal,
  645. Let him return, and flatt’ring thoughts retire;
  646. His true respect will prison false desire,
  647. And wipe the dim mist from thy doting eyne,
  648. That thou shalt see thy state, and pity mine.”
  649. “Have done,” quoth he, “my uncontrolled tide
  650. Turns not, but swells the higher by this let.
  651. Small lights are soon blown out, huge fires abide,
  652. And with the wind in greater fury fret.
  653. The petty streams that pay a daily debt
  654. To their salt sovereign, with their fresh falls’ haste
  655. Add to his flow, but alter not his taste.”
  656. “Thou art,” quoth she, “a sea, a sovereign king,
  657. And lo there falls into thy boundless flood
  658. Black lust, dishonor, shame, misgoverning,
  659. Who seek to stain the ocean of thy blood.
  660. If all these petty ills shall change thy good,
  661. Thy sea within a puddle’s womb is hearsed,
  662. And not the puddle in thy sea dispersed.
  663. So shall these slaves be king, and thou their slave;
  664. Thou nobly base, they basely dignified;
  665. Thou their fair life, and they thy fouler grave;
  666. Thou loathed in their shame, they in thy pride.
  667. The lesser thing should not the greater hide:
  668. The cedar stoops not to the base shrub’s foot,
  669. But low shrubs wither at the cedar’s root.
  670. So let thy thoughts, low vassals to thy state”—
  671. “No more,” quoth he, “by heaven, I will not hear thee.
  672. Yield to my love, if not, enforced hate,
  673. In stead of love’s coy touch, shall rudely tear thee;
  674. That done, despitefully I mean to bear thee
  675. Unto the base bed of some rascal groom,
  676. To be thy partner in this shameful doom.”
  677. This said, he sets his foot upon the light,
  678. For light and lust are deadly enemies;
  679. Shame folded up in blind concealing night,
  680. When most unseen, then most doth tyrannize.
  681. The wolf hath seiz’d his prey, the poor lamb cries,
  682. Till with her own white fleece her voice controll’d
  683. Entombs her outcry in her lips’ sweet fold.
  684. For with the nightly linen that she wears
  685. He pens her piteous clamors in her head,
  686. Cooling his hot face in the chastest tears
  687. That ever modest eyes with sorrow shed.
  688. O that prone lust should stain so pure a bed!
  689. The spots whereof could weeping purify,
  690. Her tears should drop on them perpetually.
  691. But she hath lost a dearer thing than life,
  692. And he hath won what he would lose again;
  693. This forced league doth force a further strife,
  694. This momentary joy breeds months of pain,
  695. This hot desire converts to cold disdain;
  696. Pure Chastity is rifled of her store,
  697. And Lust, the thief, far poorer than before.
  698. Look as the full-fed hound or gorged hawk,
  699. Unapt for tender smell, or speedy flight,
  700. Make slow pursuit, or altogether balk
  701. The prey wherein by nature they delight,
  702. So surfeit-taking Tarquin fares this night;
  703. His taste delicious, in digestion souring,
  704. Devours his will, that liv’d by foul devouring.
  705. O, deeper sin than bottomless conceit
  706. Can comprehend in still imagination!
  707. Drunken Desire must vomit his receipt
  708. Ere he can see his own abomination.
  709. While Lust is in his pride, no exclamation
  710. Can curb his heat, or rein his rash desire,
  711. Till like a jade, Self-will himself doth tire.
  712. And then with lank and lean discolor’d cheek,
  713. With heavy eye, knit brow, and strengthless pace,
  714. Feeble Desire, all recreant, poor, and meek,
  715. Like to a bankrupt beggar wails his case:
  716. The flesh being proud, Desire doth fight with Grace,
  717. For there it revels, and when that decays,
  718. The guilty rebel for remission prays.
  719. So fares it with this fault-full lord of Rome,
  720. Who this accomplishment so hotly chased,
  721. For now against himself he sounds this doom,
  722. That through the length of times he stands disgraced;
  723. Besides, his soul’s fair temple is defaced,
  724. To whose weak ruins muster troops of cares,
  725. To ask the spotted princess how she fares.
  726. She says her subjects with foul insurrection
  727. Have batter’d down her consecrated wall,
  728. And by their mortal fault brought in subjection
  729. Her immortality, and made her thrall
  730. To living death and pain perpetual;
  731. Which in her prescience she controlled still,
  732. But her foresight could not forestall their will.
  733. Ev’n in this thought through the dark night he stealeth,
  734. A captive victor that hath lost in gain,
  735. Bearing away the wound that nothing healeth,
  736. The scar that will despite of cure remain,
  737. Leaving his spoil perplex’d in greater pain.
  738. She bears the load of lust he left behind,
  739. And he the burthen of a guilty mind.
  740. He like a thievish dog creeps sadly thence,
  741. She like a wearied lamb lies panting there;
  742. He scowls and hates himself for his offense,
  743. She desperate with her nails her flesh doth tear;
  744. He faintly flies, sweating with guilty fear;
  745. She stays, exclaiming on the direful night,
  746. He runs, and chides his vanish’d loath’d delight.
  747. He thence departs, a heavy convertite,
  748. She there remains a hopeless castaway;
  749. He in his speed looks for the morning light,
  750. She prays she never may behold the day:
  751. “For day,” quoth she, “night’s scapes doth open lay,
  752. And my true eyes have never practic’d how
  753. To cloak offenses with a cunning brow.
  754. They think not but that every eye can see
  755. The same disgrace which they themselves behold;
  756. And therefore would they still in darkness be,
  757. To have their unseen sin remain untold;
  758. For they their guilt with weeping will unfold,
  759. And grave, like water that doth eat in steel,
  760. Upon my cheeks what helpless shame I feel.”
  761. Here she exclaims against repose and rest,
  762. And bids her eyes hereafter still be blind;
  763. She wakes her heart by beating on her breast,
  764. And bids it leap from thence, where it may find
  765. Some purer chest to close so pure a mind.
  766. Frantic with grief thus breathes she forth her spite
  767. Against the unseen secrecy of night:
  768. “O comfort-killing Night, image of hell,
  769. Dim register and notary of shame,
  770. Black stage for tragedies and murders fell,
  771. Vast sin-concealing chaos, nurse of blame!
  772. Blind muffled bawd, dark harbor for defame,
  773. Grim cave of death, whisp’ring conspirator
  774. With close-tongu’d treason and the ravisher!
  775. O hateful, vaporous, and foggy Night,
  776. Since thou art guilty of my cureless crime,
  777. Muster thy mists to meet the eastern light,
  778. Make war against proportion’d course of time;
  779. Or if thou wilt permit the sun to climb
  780. His wonted height, yet ere he go to bed,
  781. Knit poisonous clouds about his golden head.
  782. With rotten damps ravish the morning air;
  783. Let their exhal’d unwholesome breaths make sick
  784. The life of purity, the supreme fair,
  785. Ere he arrive his weary noontide prick,
  786. And let thy musty vapors march so thick
  787. That in their smoky ranks his smoth’red light
  788. May set at noon, and make perpetual night.
  789. Were Tarquin Night, as he is but Night’s child,
  790. The silver-shining queen he would distain;
  791. Her twinkling handmaids too (by him defil’d)
  792. Through Night’s black bosom should not peep again.
  793. So should I have co-partners in my pain,
  794. And fellowship in woe doth woe assuage,
  795. As palmers’ chat makes short their pilgrimage.
  796. Where now I have no one to blush with me,
  797. To cross their arms and hang their heads with mine,
  798. To mask their brows and hide their infamy,
  799. But I alone, alone must sit and pine,
  800. Seasoning the earth with show’rs of silver brine,
  801. Mingling my talk with tears, my grief with groans,
  802. Poor wasting monuments of lasting moans.
  803. O Night, thou furnace of foul reeking smoke!
  804. Let not the jealous Day behold that face,
  805. Which underneath thy black all-hiding cloak
  806. Immodestly lies martyr’d with disgrace.
  807. Keep still possession of thy gloomy place,
  808. That all the faults which in thy reign are made
  809. May likewise be sepulcher’d in thy shade.
  810. Make me not object to the tell-tale Day:
  811. The light will show, character’d in my brow,
  812. The story of sweet chastity’s decay,
  813. The impious breach of holy wedlock vow;
  814. Yea, the illiterate that know not how
  815. To cipher what is writ in learned books,
  816. Will cote my loathsome trespass in my looks.
  817. The nurse to still her child will tell my story,
  818. And fright her crying babe with Tarquin’s name;
  819. The orator to deck his oratory
  820. Will couple my reproach to Tarquin’s shame;
  821. Feast-finding minstrels, tuning my defame,
  822. Will tie the hearers to attend each line,
  823. How Tarquin wronged me, I Collatine.
  824. Let my good name, that senseless reputation,
  825. For Collatine’s dear love be kept unspotted:
  826. If that be made a theme for disputation,
  827. The branches of another root are rotted,
  828. And undeserv’d reproach to him allotted,
  829. That is as clear from this attaint of mine
  830. As I ere this was pure to Collatine.
  831. O unseen shame, invisible disgrace!
  832. O unfelt sore, crest-wounding private scar!
  833. Reproach is stamp’d in Collatinus’ face,
  834. And Tarquin’s eye may read the mot afar,
  835. How he in peace is wounded, not in war.
  836. Alas, how many bear such shameful blows,
  837. Which not themselves but he that gives them knows!
  838. If, Collatine, thine honor lay in me,
  839. From me by strong assault it is bereft:
  840. My honey lost, and I, a drone-like bee,
  841. Have no perfection of my summer left,
  842. But robb’d and ransack’d by injurious theft.
  843. In thy weak hive a wand’ring wasp hath crept,
  844. And suck’d the honey which thy chaste bee kept.
  845. Yet am I guilty of thy honor’s wrack,
  846. Yet for thy honor did I entertain him;
  847. Coming from thee, I could not put him back,
  848. For it had been dishonor to disdain him.
  849. Besides, of weariness he did complain him,
  850. And talk’d of virtue: O unlook’d-for evil,
  851. When virtue is profan’d in such a devil!
  852. Why should the worm intrude the maiden bud?
  853. Or hateful cuckoos hatch in sparrows’ nests?
  854. Or toads infect fair founts with venom mud?
  855. Or tyrant folly lurk in gentle breasts?
  856. Or kings be breakers of their own behests?
  857. But no perfection is so absolute,
  858. That some impurity doth not pollute.
  859. The aged man that coffers up his gold
  860. Is plagu’d with cramps and gouts and painful fits,
  861. And scarce hath eyes his treasure to behold,
  862. But like still-pining Tantalus he sits,
  863. And useless barns the harvest of his wits;
  864. Having no other pleasure of his gain
  865. But torment that it cannot cure his pain.
  866. So then he hath it when he cannot use it,
  867. And leaves it to be mast’red by his young,
  868. Who in their pride do presently abuse it;
  869. Their father was too weak, and they too strong,
  870. To hold their cursed-blessed fortune long.
  871. The sweets we wish for turn to loathed sours
  872. Even in the moment that we call them ours.
  873. Unruly blasts wait on the tender spring,
  874. Unwholesome weeds take root with precious flow’rs,
  875. The adder hisses where the sweet birds sing,
  876. What virtue breeds iniquity devours:
  877. We have no good that we can say is ours,
  878. But ill-annexed Opportunity
  879. Or kills his life or else his quality.
  880. O Opportunity, thy guilt is great!
  881. ’Tis thou that execut’st the traitor’s treason;
  882. Thou sets the wolf where he the lamb may get;
  883. Whoever plots the sin, thou ’point’st the season;
  884. ’Tis thou that spurn’st at right, at law, at reason,
  885. And in thy shady cell, where none may spy him,
  886. Sits Sin, to seize the souls that wander by him.
  887. Thou makest the vestal violate her oath,
  888. Thou blowest the fire when temperance is thaw’d,
  889. Thou smother’st honesty, thou murd’rest troth,
  890. Thou foul abettor, thou notorious bawd,
  891. Thou plantest scandal, and displacest laud.
  892. Thou ravisher, thou traitor, thou false thief,
  893. Thy honey turns to gall, thy joy to grief.
  894. Thy secret pleasure turns to open shame,
  895. Thy private feasting to a public fast,
  896. Thy smoothing titles to a ragged name,
  897. Thy sug’red tongue to bitter wormwood taste;
  898. Thy violent vanities can never last.
  899. How comes it then, vile Opportunity,
  900. Being so bad, such numbers seek for thee?
  901. When wilt thou be the humble suppliant’s friend,
  902. And bring him where his suit may be obtained?
  903. When wilt thou sort an hour great strifes to end?
  904. Or free that soul which wretchedness hath chained?
  905. Give physic to the sick, ease to the pained?
  906. The poor, lame, blind, halt, creep, cry out for thee,
  907. But they ne’er meet with Opportunity.
  908. The patient dies while the physician sleeps,
  909. The orphan pines while the oppressor feeds,
  910. Justice is feasting while the widow weeps,
  911. Advice is sporting while infection breeds.
  912. Thou grant’st no time for charitable deeds.
  913. Wrath, envy, treason, rape, and murder’s rages,
  914. Thy heinous hours wait on them as their pages.
  915. When Truth and Virtue have to do with thee,
  916. A thousand crosses keep them from thy aid:
  917. They buy thy help, but Sin ne’er gives a fee,
  918. He gratis comes, and thou art well apaid,
  919. As well to hear as grant what he hath said.
  920. My Collatine would else have come to me
  921. When Tarquin did, but he was stay’d by thee.
  922. Guilty thou art of murder and of theft,
  923. Guilty of perjury and subornation,
  924. Guilty of treason, forgery, and shift,
  925. Guilty of incest, that abomination;
  926. An accessary by thine inclination
  927. To all sins past and all that are to come,
  928. From the creation to the general doom.
  929. Misshapen Time, copesmate of ugly Night,
  930. Swift subtle post, carrier of grisly care,
  931. Eater of youth, false slave to false delight,
  932. base watch of woes, sin’s pack-horse, virtue’s snare!
  933. Thou nursest all, and murd’rest all that are.
  934. O, hear me then, injurious shifting Time,
  935. Be guilty of my death, since of my crime.
  936. Why hath thy servant Opportunity
  937. Betray’d the hours thou gav’st me to repose?
  938. Cancell’d my fortunes, and enchained me
  939. To endless date of never-ending woes?
  940. Time’s office is to fine the hate of foes,
  941. To eat up errors by opinion bred,
  942. Not spend the dowry of a lawful bed.
  943. Time’s glory is to calm contending kings,
  944. To unmask falsehood, and bring truth to light,
  945. To stamp the seal of time in aged things,
  946. To wake the morn, and sentinel the night,
  947. To wrong the wronger till he render right,
  948. To ruinate proud buildings with thy hours,
  949. And smear with dust their glitt’ring golden tow’rs;
  950. To fill with worm-holes stately monuments,
  951. To feed oblivion with decay of things,
  952. To blot old books, and alter their contents,
  953. To pluck the quills from ancient ravens’ wings,
  954. To dry the old oak’s sap, and cherish springs,
  955. To spoil antiquities of hammer’d steel,
  956. And turn the giddy round of Fortune’s wheel;
  957. To show the beldame daughters of her daughter,
  958. To make the child a man, the man a child,
  959. To slay the tiger that doth live by slaughter,
  960. To tame the unicorn and lion wild,
  961. To mock the subtle in themselves beguil’d,
  962. To cheer the ploughman with increaseful crops,
  963. And waste huge stones with little water-drops.
  964. Why work’st thou mischief in thy pilgrimage,
  965. Unless thou couldst return to make amends?
  966. One poor retiring minute in an age
  967. Would purchase thee a thousand thousand friends,
  968. Lending him wit that to bad debtors lends:
  969. O this dread night, wouldst thou one hour come back,
  970. I could prevent this storm, and shun thy wrack!
  971. Thou ceaseless lackey to eternity,
  972. With some mischance cross Tarquin in his flight.
  973. Devise extremes beyond extremity,
  974. To make him curse this cursed crimeful night.
  975. Let ghastly shadows his lewd eyes affright,
