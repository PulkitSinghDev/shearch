The Two Gentlemen of Verona

Act 1

Scene 1

Verona. An open place.

Enter Valentine, Proteus. VAL. PRO.
  1. Cease to persuade, my loving Proteus:
  2. Home-keeping youth have ever homely wits.
  3. Were’t not affection chains thy tender days
  4. To the sweet glances of thy honor’d love,
  5. I rather would entreat thy company,
  6. To see the wonders of the world abroad,
  7. Than (living dully sluggardiz’d at home)
  8. Wear out thy youth with shapeless idleness.
  9. But since thou lov’st, love still, and thrive therein,
  10. Even as I would, when I to love begin.
  1. Wilt thou be gone? Sweet Valentine, adieu,
  2. Think on thy Proteus, when thou, happ’ly, seest
  3. Some rare noteworthy object in thy travel.
  4. Wish me partaker in thy happiness
  5. When thou dost meet good hap; and in thy danger
  6. (If ever danger do environ thee)
  7. Commend thy grievance to my holy prayers,
  8. For I will be thy beadsman, Valentine.
  1. And on a love-book pray for my success?
  1. Upon some book I love I’ll pray for thee.
  1. That’s on some shallow story of deep love,
  2. How young Leander cross’d the Hellespont.
  1. That’s a deep story of a deeper love,
  2. For he was more than over shoes in love.
  1. ’Tis true; for you are over boots in love,
  2. And yet you never swom the Hellespont.
  1. Over the boots? Nay, give me not the boots.
  1. No, I will not; for it boots thee not.
  1. What?
  1. To be in love—where scorn is bought with groans;
  2. Coy looks with heart-sore sighs; one fading moment’s mirth
  3. With twenty watchful, weary, tedious nights:
  4. If happ’ly won, perhaps a hapless gain;
  5. If lost, why then a grievous labor won;
  6. However—but a folly bought with wit,
  7. Or else a wit by folly vanquished.
  1. So, by your circumstance, you call me fool.
  1. So, by your circumstance, I fear you’ll prove.
  1. ’Tis love you cavil at, I am not Love.
  1. Love is your master, for he masters you;
  2. And he that is so yoked by a fool,
  3. Methinks should not be chronicled for wise.
  1. Yet writers say: as in the sweetest bud
  2. The eating canker dwells, so eating love
  3. Inhabits in the finest wits of all.
  1. And writers say: as the most forward bud
  2. Is eaten by the canker ere it blow,
  3. Even so by love the young and tender wit
  4. Is turn’d to folly, blasting in the bud,
  5. Losing his verdure, even in the prime,
  6. And all the fair effects of future hopes.
  7. But wherefore waste I time to counsel thee
  8. That art a votary to fond desire?
  9. Once more adieu. My father at the road
  10. Expects my coming, there to see me shipp’d.
  1. And thither will I bring thee, Valentine.
  1. Sweet Proteus, no; now let us take our leave.
  2. To Milan let me hear from thee by letters
  3. Of thy success in love, and what news else
  4. Betideth here in absence of thy friend;
  5. And I likewise will visit thee with mine.
  1. All happiness bechance to thee in Milan.
  1. As much to you at home; and so farewell.
Exit. VAL.
  1. He after honor hunts, I after love:
  2. He leaves his friends, to dignify them more;
  3. I leave myself, my friends, and all, for love.
  4. Thou, Julia, thou hast metamorphis’d me,
  5. Made me neglect my studies, lose my time,
  6. War with good counsel, set the world at nought;
  7. Made wit with musing weak, heart sick with thought.
Enter Speed. SPEED.
  1. Sir Proteus! ’Save you! Saw you my master?
  1. But now he parted hence to embark for Milan.
  1. Twenty to one then he is shipp’d already,
  2. And I have play’d the sheep in losing him.
  1. Indeed a sheep doth very often stray,
  2. And if the shepherd be awhile away.
  1. You conclude that my master is a shepherd then, and I a sheep?
  1. I do.
  1. Why then my horns are his horns, whether I wake or sleep.
  1. A silly answer, and fitting well a sheep.
  1. This proves me still a sheep.
  1. True; and thy master a shepherd.
  1. Nay, that I can deny by a circumstance.
  1. It shall go hard but I’ll prove it by another.
  1. The shepherd seeks the sheep, and not the sheep the shepherd; but I seek my master, and my master seeks not me: therefore I am no sheep.
  1. The sheep for fodder follow the shepherd, the shepherd for food follows not the sheep; thou for wages followest thy master, thy master for wages follows not thee: therefore thou art a sheep.
  1. Such another proof will make me cry “baa.”
  1. But dost thou hear? Gav’st thou my letter to Julia?
  1. Ay, sir; I (a lost mutton) gave your letter to her (a lac’d mutton), and she (a lac’d mutton) gave me (a lost mutton) nothing for my labor.
  1. Here’s too small a pasture for such store of muttons.
  1. If the ground be overcharg’d, you were best stick her.
  1. Nay, in that you are astray; ’twere best pound you.
  1. Nay, sir, less than a pound shall serve me for carrying your letter.
  1. You mistake; I mean the pound—a pinfold.
  1. From a pound to a pin? Fold it over and over,
  2. ’Tis threefold too little for carrying a letter to your lover.
  1. But what said she?
Speed nods, and Proteus looks at him questioningly. SPEED. PRO.
  1. Ay.
  1. Nod-ay—why, that’s “noddy.”
  1. You mistook, sir: I say, she did nod; and you ask me if she did nod, and I say, “Ay.”
  1. And that set together is “noddy.”
  1. Now you have taken the pains to set it together, take it for your pains.
  1. No, no, you shall have it for bearing the letter.
  1. Well, I perceive I must be fain to bear with you.
  1. Why, sir, how do you bear with me?
  1. Marry, sir, the letter, very orderly, having nothing but the word “noddy” for my pains.
  1. Beshrew me, but you have a quick wit.
  1. And yet it cannot overtake your slow purse.
  1. Come, come, open the matter in brief: what said she?
  1. Open your purse, that the money and the matter may be both at once deliver’d.
  1. Well, sir, here is for your pains. What said she?
  1. Truly, sir, I think you’ll hardly win her.
  1. Why? Couldst thou perceive so much from her?
  1. Sir, I could perceive nothing at all from her; no, not so much as a ducat for delivering your letter: and being so hard to me that brought your mind, I fear she’ll prove as hard to you in telling your mind. Give her no token but stones, for she’s as hard as steel.
  1. What said she? Nothing?
  1. No, not so much as “Take this for thy pains.” To testify your bounty, I thank you, you have testern’d me; in requital whereof, henceforth carry your letters yourself: and so, sir, I’ll commend you to my master.
  1. Go, go, be gone, to save your ship from wrack,
  2. Which cannot perish having thee aboard,
  3. Being destin’d to a drier death on shore.
  4. Exit Speed. SPEED.
  5. I must go send some better messenger:
  6. I fear my Julia would not deign my lines,
  7. Receiving them from such a worthless post.
Exit. PRO.

Scene 2

Verona. The garden of Julia’s house.

Enter Julia and Lucetta. JUL. LUC.
  1. But say, Lucetta, now we are alone,
  2. Wouldst thou then counsel me to fall in love?
  1. Ay, madam, so you stumble not unheedfully.
  1. Of all the fair resort of gentlemen
  2. That every day with parle encounter me,
  3. In thy opinion which is worthiest love?
  1. Please you repeat their names, I’ll show my mind
  2. According to my shallow simple skill.
  1. What think’st thou of the fair Sir Eglamour?
  1. As of a knight well-spoken, neat, and fine;
  2. But were I you, he never should be mine.
  1. What think’st thou of the rich Mercatio?
  1. Well of his wealth; but of himself, so, so.
  1. What think’st thou of the gentle Proteus?
  1. Lord, Lord! To see what folly reigns in us!
  1. How now? What means this passion at his name?
  1. Pardon, dear madam, ’tis a passing shame
  2. That I (unworthy body as I am)
  3. Should censure thus on lovely gentlemen.
  1. Why not on Proteus, as of all the rest?
  1. Then thus: of many good I think him best.
  1. Your reason?
  1. I have no other but a woman’s reason:
  2. I think him so, because I think him so.
  1. And wouldst thou have me cast my love on him?
  1. Ay—if you thought your love not cast away.
  1. Why, he, of all the rest, hath never mov’d me.
  1. Yet he, of all the rest, I think best loves ye.
  1. His little speaking shows his love but small.
  1. Fire that’s closest kept burns most of all.
  1. They do not love that do not show their love.
  1. O, they love least that let men know their love.
  1. I would I knew his mind.
  1. Peruse this paper, madam.
  1. “To Julia”—say, from whom?
  1. That the contents will show.
  1. Say, say; who gave it thee?
  1. Sir Valentine’s page; and sent, I think, from Proteus.
  2. He would have given it you, but I, being in the way,
  3. Did in your name receive it; pardon the fault, I pray.
  1. Now, by my modesty, a goodly broker!
  2. Dare you presume to harbor wanton lines?
  3. To whisper and conspire against my youth?
  4. Now trust me, ’tis an office of great worth,
  5. And you an officer fit for the place.
  6. There! Take the paper; see it be return’d,
  7. Or else return no more into my sight.
  1. To plead for love deserves more fee than hate.
  1. Will ye be gone?
  1. That you may ruminate.
Exit. LUC.
  1. And yet I would I had o’erlook’d the letter;
  2. It were a shame to call her back again,
  3. And pray her to a fault for which I chid her.
  4. What ’fool is she, that knows I am a maid,
  5. And would not force the letter to my view!
  6. Since maids, in modesty, say “no” to that
  7. Which they would have the profferer construe “ay.”
  8. Fie, fie, how wayward is this foolish love,
  9. That (like a testy babe) will scratch the nurse
  10. And presently, all humbled, kiss the rod!
  11. How churlishly I chid Lucetta hence,
  12. When willingly I would have had her here!
  13. How angerly I taught my brow to frown,
  14. When inward joy enforc’d my heart to smile!
  15. My penance is, to call Lucetta back
  16. And ask remission for my folly past.
  17. What ho! Lucetta!
Enter Lucetta. LUC.
  1. What would your ladyship?
  1. Is’t near dinner-time?
  1. I would it were,
  2. That you might kill your stomach on your meat,
  3. And not upon your maid.
  1. What is’t that you
  2. Took up so gingerly?
  1. Nothing.
  1. Why didst thou stoop then?
  1. To take a paper up that I let fall.
  1. And is that paper nothing?
  1. Nothing concerning me.
  1. Then let it lie for those that it concerns.
  1. Madam, it will not lie where it concerns
  2. Unless it have a false interpreter.
  1. Some love of yours hath writ to you in rhyme.
  1. That I might sing it, madam, to a tune:
  2. Give me a note, your ladyship can set.
  1. As little by such toys as may be possible:
  2. Best sing it to the tune of “Light o’ love.”
  1. It is too heavy for so light a tune.
  1. Heavy? Belike it hath some burden then?
  1. Ay; and melodious were it, would you sing it.
  1. And why not you?
  1. I cannot reach so high.
  1. Let’s see your song.
  2. Takes the letter. JUL.
  3. How now, minion?
  1. Keep tune there still, so you will sing it out.
  2. And yet methinks I do not like this tune.
  1. You do not?
  1. No, madam, ’tis too sharp.
  1. You, minion, are too saucy.
  1. Nay, now you are too flat,
  2. And mar the concord with too harsh a descant:
  3. There wanteth but a mean to fill your song.
  1. The mean is drown’d with your unruly bass.
  1. Indeed I bid the base for Proteus.
  1. This babble shall not henceforth trouble me.
  2. Here is a coil with protestation!
  3. Tears the letter. JUL.
  4. Go, get you gone; and let the papers lie:
  5. You would be fing’ring them, to anger me.
  1. She makes it strange, but she would be best pleas’d
  2. To be so ang’red with another letter.
Exit. LUC.
  1. Nay, would I were so ang’red with the same.
  2. O hateful hands, to tear such loving words!
  3. Injurious wasps, to feed on such sweet honey,
  4. And kill the bees that yield it with your stings!
  5. I’ll kiss each several paper for amends.
  6. Look, here is writ “kind Julia.” Unkind Julia,
  7. As in revenge of thy ingratitude,
  8. I throw thy name against the bruising stones,
  9. Trampling contemptuously on thy disdain.
  10. And here is writ “love-wounded Proteus.”
  11. Poor wounded name: my bosom as a bed
  12. Shall lodge thee till thy wound be throughly heal’d;
  13. And thus I search it with a sovereign kiss.
  14. But twice, or thrice, was “Proteus” written down:
  15. Be calm, good wind, blow not a word away
  16. Till I have found each letter in the letter,
  17. Except mine own name; that, some whirlwind bear
  18. Unto a ragged, fearful, hanging rock,
  19. And throw it thence into the raging sea.
  20. Lo, here in one line is his name twice writ,
  21. “Poor forlorn Proteus, passionate Proteus:
  22. To the sweet Julia”—that I’ll tear away—
  23. And yet I will not, sith so prettily
  24. He couples it to his complaining names.
  25. Thus will I fold them one upon another;
  26. Now kiss, embrace, contend, do what you will.
Enter Lucetta. LUC.
  1. Madam,
  2. Dinner is ready, and your father stays.
  1. Well, let us go.
  1. What, shall these papers lie like tell-tales here?
  1. If you respect them, best to take them up.
  1. Nay, I was taken up for laying them down;
  2. Yet here they shall not lie, for catching cold.
  1. I see you have a month’s mind to them.
  1. Ay, madam, you may say what sights you see;
  2. I see things too, although you judge I wink.
  1. Come, come, will’t please you go?
Exeunt. JUL. LUC.

Scene 3

Verona. A room in Antonio’s house.

Enter Antonio and Panthino. ANT. PAN.
  1. Tell me, Panthino, what sad talk was that
  2. Wherewith my brother held you in the cloister?
  1. ’Twas of his nephew Proteus, your son.
  1. Why, what of him?
  1. He wond’red that your lordship
  2. Would suffer him to spend his youth at home,
  3. While other men, of slender reputation,
  4. Put forth their sons to seek preferment out:
  5. Some to the wars, to try their fortune there;
  6. Some to discover islands far away;
  7. Some to the studious universities.
  8. For any or for all these exercises
  9. He said that Proteus, your son, was meet;
  10. And did request me to importune you
  11. To let him spend his time no more at home,
  12. Which would be great impeachment to his age,
  13. In having known no travel in his youth.
  1. Nor need’st thou much importune me to that
  2. Whereon this month I have been hammering.
  3. I have consider’d well his loss of time,
  4. And how he cannot be a perfect man,
  5. Not being tried and tutor’d in the world:
  6. Experience is by industry achiev’d,
  7. And perfected by the swift course of time.
  8. Then tell me, whither were I best to send him?
  1. I think your lordship is not ignorant
  2. How his companion, youthful Valentine,
  3. Attends the Emperor in his royal court.
  1. I know it well.
  1. ’Twere good, I think, your lordship sent him thither:
  2. There shall he practice tilts and tournaments,
  3. Hear sweet discourse, converse with noblemen,
  4. And be in eye of every exercise
  5. Worthy his youth and nobleness of birth.
  1. I like thy counsel; well hast thou advis’d;
  2. And that thou mayst perceive how well I like it,
  3. The execution of it shall make known:
  4. Even with the speediest expedition
  5. I will dispatch him to the Emperor’s court.
  1. Tomorrow, may it please you, Don Alphonso
  2. With other gentlemen of good esteem
  3. Are journeying to salute the Emperor,
  4. And to commend their service to his will.
  1. Good company; with them shall Proteus go—
  2. Enter Proteus. PRO.
  3. And in good time! Now will we break with him.
  1. Sweet love, sweet lines, sweet life!
  2. Here is her hand, the agent of her heart;
  3. Here is her oath for love, her honor’s pawn:
  4. O that our fathers would applaud our loves,
  5. To seal our happiness with their consents!
  6. O heavenly Julia!
  1. How now? What letter are you reading there?
  1. May’t please your lordship, ’tis a word or two
  2. Of commendations sent from Valentine,
  3. Deliver’d by a friend that came from him.
  1. Lend me the letter; let me see what news.
  1. There is no news, my lord, but that he writes
  2. How happily he lives, how well-belov’d
  3. And daily graced by the Emperor;
  4. Wishing me with him, partner of his fortune.
  1. And how stand you affected to his wish?
  1. As one relying on your lordship’s will,
  2. And not depending on his friendly wish.
  1. My will is something sorted with his wish:
  2. Muse not that I thus suddenly proceed;
  3. For what I will, I will, and there an end.
  4. I am resolv’d that thou shalt spend some time
  5. With Valentinus in the Emperor’s court;
  6. What maintenance he from his friends receives,
  7. Like exhibition thou shalt have from me.
  8. Tomorrow be in readiness to go—
  9. Excuse it not, for I am peremptory.
  1. My lord I cannot be so soon provided:
  2. Please you deliberate a day or two.
  1. Look what thou want’st shall be sent after thee.
  2. No more of stay: tomorrow thou must go.
  3. Come on, Panthino; you shall be employ’d
  4. To hasten on his expedition.
Exeunt Antonio and Panthino. ANT. PAN.
  1. Thus have I shunn’d the fire for fear of burning,
  2. And drench’d me in the sea, where I am drown’d.
  3. I fear’d to show my father Julia’s letter,
  4. Lest he should take exceptions to my love,
  5. And with the vantage of mine own excuse
  6. Hath he excepted most against my love.
  7. O, how this spring of love resembleth
  8. The uncertain glory of an April day,
  9. Which now shows all the beauty of the sun,
  10. And by and by a cloud takes all away.
Enter Panthino. PAN.
  1. Sir Proteus, your father calls for you:
  2. He is in haste; therefore I pray you go.
  1. Why, this it is: my heart accords thereto,
  2. And yet a thousand times it answers “no.”
Exeunt. PRO. PAN.

Act 2

Scene 1

Milan. A room in the Duke’s palace.

Enter Valentine, Speed. VAL. SPEED.
  1. Sir, your glove.
  1. Not mine: my gloves are on.
  1. Why then this may be yours—for this is but one.
  1. Ha? Let me see; ay, give it me, it’s mine:
  2. Sweet ornament that decks a thing divine—
  3. Ah, Silvia, Silvia!
  1. Shouting. SPEED.
  2. Madam Silvia! Madam Silvia!
  1. How now, sirrah?
  1. She is not within hearing, sir.
  1. Why, sir, who bade you call her?
  1. Your worship, sir, or else I mistook.
  1. Well—You’ll still be too forward.
  1. And yet I was last chidden for being too slow.
  1. Go to, sir; tell me, do you know Madam Silvia?
  1. She that your worship loves?
  1. Why, how know you that I am in love?
  1. Marry, by these special marks: first, you have learn’d, like Sir Proteus, to wreathe your arms, like a malcontent; to relish a love-song, like a robin-redbreast; to walk alone, like one that had the pestilence; to sigh, like a schoolboy that had lost his A B C; to weep, like a young wench that had buried her grandam; to fast, like one that takes diet; to watch, like one that fears robbing; to speak puling, like a beggar at Hallowmas. You were wont, when you laugh’d, to crow like a cock; when you walk’d, to walk like one of the lions; when you fasted, it was presently after dinner; when you look’d sadly, it was for want of money: and now you are metamorphis’d with a mistress, that when I look on you, I can hardly think you my master.
  1. Are all these things perceiv’d in me?
  1. They are all perceiv’d without ye.
  1. Without me? They cannot.
  1. Without you? Nay, that’s certain; for without you were so simple, none else would: but you are so without these follies, that these follies are within you, and shine through you like the water in an urinal, that not an eye that sees you but is a physician to comment on your malady.
  1. But tell me: dost thou know my lady Silvia?
  1. She that you gaze on so as she sits at supper?
  1. Hast thou observ’d that? Even she I mean.
  1. Why, sir, I know her not.
  1. Dost thou know her by my gazing on her, and yet know’st her not?
  1. Is she not hard-favor’d, sir?
  1. Not so fair, boy, as well-favor’d.
  1. Sir, I know that well enough.
  1. What dost thou know?
  1. That she is not so fair as (of you) well favor’d.
  1. I mean that her beauty is exquisite, but her favor infinite.
  1. That’s because the one is painted, and the other out of all count.
  1. How painted? And how out of count?
  1. Marry, sir, so painted to make her fair, that no man counts of her beauty.
  1. How esteem’st thou me? I account of her beauty.
  1. You never saw her since she was deform’d.
  1. How long hath she been deform’d?
  1. Ever since you lov’d her.
  1. I have lov’d her ever since I saw her, and still I see her beautiful.
  1. If you love her, you cannot see her.
  1. Why?
  1. Because Love is blind. O that you had mine eyes, or your own eyes had the lights they were wont to have when you chid at Sir Proteus for going ungarter’d!
  1. What should I see then?
  1. Your own present folly, and her passing deformity: for he, being in love, could not see to garter his hose; and you, being in love, cannot see to put on your hose.
  1. Belike, boy, then you are in love—for last morning you could not see to wipe my shoes.
  1. True, sir; I was in love with my bed. I thank you, you swing’d me for my love, which makes me the bolder to chide you for yours.
  1. In conclusion, I stand affected to her.
  1. I would you were set, so your affection would cease.
  1. Last night she enjoin’d me to write some lines to one she loves.
  1. And have you?
  1. I have.
  1. Are they not lamely writ?
  1. No, boy, but as well as I can do them.
  2. Enter Silvia. SIL.
  3. Peace, here she comes.
  1. Aside. SPEED.
  2. O excellent motion! O exceeding puppet! Now will he interpret to her.
  1. Madam and mistress, a thousand good morrows.
  1. Aside. SPEED.
  2. O, give ye good ev’n! Here’s a million of manners.
  1. Sir Valentine and servant, to you two thousand.
  1. Aside. SPEED.
  2. He should give her interest, and she gives it him.
  1. As you enjoin’d me, I have writ your letter
  2. Unto the secret, nameless friend of yours;
  3. Which I was much unwilling to proceed in,
  4. But for my duty to your ladyship.
  1. I thank you, gentle servant—’tis very clerkly done.
  1. Now trust me, madam, it came hardly off;
  2. For being ignorant to whom it goes,
  3. I writ at random, very doubtfully.
  1. Perchance you think too much of so much pains?
  1. No, madam; so it stead you, I will write
  2. (Please you command) a thousand times as much;
  3. And yet—
  1. A pretty period! Well—I guess the sequel;
  2. And yet I will not name it—and yet I care not—
  3. And yet take this again—and yet I thank you—
  4. Meaning henceforth to trouble you no more.
  1. Aside. SPEED.
  2. And yet you will; and yet another “yet.”
  1. What means your ladyship? Do you not like it?
  1. Yes, yes; the lines are very quaintly writ,
  2. But (since unwillingly) take them again.
  3. Nay, take them.
  1. Madam, they are for you.
  1. Ay, ay; you writ them, sir, at my request,
  2. But I will none of them; they are for you.
  3. I would have had them writ more movingly.
  1. Please you, I’ll write your ladyship another.
  1. And when it’s writ, for my sake read it over,
  2. And if it please you, so; if not, why, so.
  1. If it please me, madam, what then?
  1. Why, if it please you, take it for your labor;
  2. And so good morrow, servant.
Exit Silvia. SIL.
  1. O jest unseen, inscrutable; invisible,
  2. As a nose on a man’s face, or a weathercock on a steeple!
  3. My master sues to her; and she hath taught her suitor,
  4. He being her pupil, to become her tutor.
  5. O excellent device, was there ever heard a better,
  6. That my master being scribe, to himself should write the letter?
  1. How now, sir? What are you reasoning with yourself?
  1. Nay, I was rhyming; ’tis you that have the reason.
  1. To do what?
  1. To be a spokesman from Madam Silvia.

Scene 3

Verona. A street.

Enter Launce, leading a dog. LAUNCE. CRAB
  1. Nay, ’twill be this hour ere I have done weeping; all the kind of the Launces have this very fault. I have receiv’d my proportion, like the prodigious son, and am going with Sir Proteus to the Imperial’s court. I think Crab my dog be the sourest-natur’d dog that lives: my mother weeping, my father wailing, my sister crying, our maid howling, our cat wringing her hands, and all our house in a great perplexity, yet did not this cruel-hearted cur shed one tear. He is a stone, a very pebble stone, and has no more pity in him than a dog. A Jew would have wept to have seen our parting; why, my grandam, having no eyes, look you, wept herself blind at my parting. Nay, I’ll show you the manner of it. This shoe is my father; no, this left shoe is my father; no, no, this left shoe is my mother; nay, that cannot be so neither; yes, it is so, it is so—it hath the worser sole. This shoe, with the hole in it, is my mother, and this my father—a vengeance on’t! There ’tis. Now, sir, this staff is my sister, for, look you, she is as white as a lily and as small as a wand. This hat is Nan, our maid. I am the dog—no, the dog is himself, and I am the dog—O! The dog is me, and I am myself; ay, so, so. Now come I to my father: “Father, your blessing.” Now should not the shoe speak a word for weeping; now should I kiss my father; well, he weeps on. Now come I to my mother. O that she could speak now like a wood woman! Well, I kiss her; why, there ’tis; here’s my mother’s breath up and down. Now come I to my sister; mark the moan she makes. Now the dog all this while sheds not a tear, nor speaks a word; but see how I lay the dust with my tears.
Enter Panthino. PAN.
  1. Launce, away, away! Aboard! Thy master is shipp’d, and thou art to post after with oars. What’s the matter? Why weep’st thou, man? Away, ass, you’ll lose the tide, if you tarry any longer.

  1. What’s the unkindest tide?
  1. Why, he that’s tied here, Crab, my dog.

  1. For fear thou shouldst lose thy tongue.
  1. Where should I lose my tongue?
  1. In thy tale.
  1. In thy tail!
  1. Lose the tide, and the voyage, and the master, and the service, and the tied! Why, man, if the river were dry, I am able to fill it with my tears; if the wind were down, I could drive the boat with my sighs.
  1. Come; come away, man—I was sent to call thee.
  1. Sir—call me what thou dar’st.
  1. Wilt thou go?
  1. Well, I will go.
Exeunt. LAUNCE. CRAB PAN.

  1. Happ’ly I do.
  1. So do counterfeits.
  1. So do you.

  1. I quote it in your jerkin.

  1. Well then I’ll double your folly.
  1. How?

  1. No more, gentlemen, no more; here comes my father.
Enter Duke. DUKE.
  1. Now, daughter Silvia, you are hard beset.

  3. What say you to a letter from your friends

  2. We have convers’d and spent our hours together,

  7. Made use and fair advantage of his days;
  8. His years but young, but his experience old;
  9. His head unmellowed, but his judgment ripe;
  10. And in a word (for far behind his worth
  11. Comes all the praises that I now bestow),
  12. He is complete in feature and in mind
  13. With all good grace to grace a gentleman.

  2. He is as worthy for an empress’ love
  3. As meet to be an emperor’s counsellor.
  4. Well, sir—this gentleman is come to me
  5. With commendation from great potentates,
  6. And here he means to spend his time a while.
  7. I think ’tis no unwelcome news to you.
  1. Should I have wish’d a thing, it had been he.
  1. Welcome him then according to his worth—
  2. Silvia, I speak to you, and you, Sir Thurio;
  3. For Valentine, I need not cite him to it.

Exit. DUKE.

  2. Had come along with me, but that his mistress
  3. Did hold his eyes lock’d in her crystal looks.

  2. Upon some other pawn for fealty.

  2. How could he see his way to seek out you?
  1. Why, lady, Love hath twenty pair of eyes.
  1. They say that Love hath not an eye at all.
  1. To see such lovers, Thurio, as yourself:
  2. Upon a homely object Love can wink.
  1. Have done, have done; here comes the gentleman.
Exit Thurio. THU.

  1. Welcome, dear Proteus! Mistress, I beseech you
  2. Confirm his welcome with some special favor.

  2. If this be he you oft have wish’d to hear from.

  1. Too low a mistress for so high a servant.
  1. Not so, sweet lady, but too mean a servant

  1. And duty never yet did want his meed.
  2. Servant, you are welcome to a worthless mistress.
  1. I’ll die on him that says so but yourself.

  1. That you are worthless.
Enter Thurio. THU.
  1. Madam, my lord your father would speak with you.
  1. I wait upon his pleasure. Come, Sir Thurio,
  2. Go with me. Once more, new servant, welcome;
  3. I’ll leave you to confer of home affairs;
  4. When you have done, we look to hear from you.
  1. We’ll both attend upon your ladyship.
Exeunt Silvia and Thurio. SIL. THU.
  1. Now tell me: how do all from whence you came?
  1. Your friends are well and have them much commended.
  1. And how do yours?
  1. I left them all in health.
  1. How does your lady, and how thrives your love?
  1. My tales of love were wont to weary you;
  2. I know you joy not in a love-discourse.
  1. Ay, Proteus, but that life is alter’d now:
  2. I have done penance for contemning Love,
  3. Whose high imperious thoughts have punish’d me
  4. With bitter fasts, with penitential groans,
  5. With nightly tears, and daily heart-sore sighs,
  6. For in revenge of my contempt of love,
  7. Love hath chas’d sleep from my enthralled eyes,
  8. And made them watchers of mine own heart’s sorrow.
  9. O gentle Proteus, Love’s a mighty lord,
  10. And hath so humbled me as I confess
  11. There is no woe to his correction,
  12. Nor to his service no such joy on earth:
  13. Now no discourse, except it be of love;
  14. Now can I break my fast, dine, sup, and sleep,
  15. Upon the very naked name of love.
  1. Enough; I read your fortune in your eye.
  2. Was this the idol that you worship so?
  1. Even she; and is she not a heavenly saint?
  1. No; but she is an earthly paragon.
  1. Call her divine.
  1. I will not flatter her.
  1. O, flatter me; for love delights in praises.
  1. When I was sick, you gave me bitter pills,
  2. And I must minister the like to you.
  1. Then speak the truth by her; if not divine,
  2. Yet let her be a principality,
  3. Sovereign to all the creatures on the earth.
  1. Except my mistress.
  1. Sweet, except not any,
  2. Except thou wilt except against my love.
  1. Have I not reason to prefer mine own?
  1. And I will help thee to prefer her too:
  2. She shall be dignified with this high honor—
  3. To bear my lady’s train, lest the base earth
  4. Should from her vesture chance to steal a kiss,
  5. And of so great a favor growing proud,
  6. Disdain to root the summer-swelling flow’r,
  7. And make rough winter everlastingly.
  1. Why, Valentine, what braggadism is this?
  1. Pardon me, Proteus, all I can is nothing
  2. To her, whose worth makes other worthies nothing:
  3. She is alone.
  1. Then let her alone.
  1. Not for the world. Why, man, she is mine own,
  2. And I as rich in having such a jewel
  3. As twenty seas, if all their sand were pearl,
  4. The water nectar, and the rocks pure gold.
  5. Forgive me, that I do not dream on thee,
  6. Because thou seest me dote upon my love.
  7. My foolish rival, that her father likes
  8. (Only for his possessions are so huge),
  9. Is gone with her along, and I must after,
  10. For love, thou know’st, is full of jealousy.
  1. But she loves you?
  1. Ay, and we are betroth’d: nay more, our marriage hour,
  2. With all the cunning manner of our flight,
  3. Determin’d of—how I must climb her window,
  4. The ladder made of cords, and all the means
  5. Plotted and ’greed on for my happiness.
  6. Good Proteus, go with me to my chamber,
  7. In these affairs to aid me with thy counsel.
  1. Go on before; I shall inquire you forth.
  2. I must unto the road, to disembark
  3. Some necessaries that I needs must use,
  4. And then I’ll presently attend you.
  1. Will you make haste?
  1. I will.
  2. Exit Valentine. VAL.
  3. Even as one heat another heat expels,
  4. Or as one nail by strength drives out another,
  5. So the remembrance of my former love
  6. Is by a newer object quite forgotten.
  7. Is it mine eye, or Valentinus’ praise,
  8. Her true perfection, or my false transgression,
  9. That makes me reasonless, to reason thus?
  10. She is fair; and so is Julia that I love
  11. (That I did love, for now my love is thaw’d,
  12. Which like a waxen image ’gainst a fire
  13. Bears no impression of the thing it was).
  14. Methinks my zeal to Valentine is cold,
  15. And that I love him not as I was wont:
  16. O, but I love his lady too too much,
  17. And that’s the reason I love him so little.
  18. How shall I dote on her with more advice,
  19. That thus without advice begin to love her?
  20. ’Tis but her picture I have yet beheld,
  21. And that hath dazzled my reason’s light;
  22. But when I look on her perfections,
  23. There is no reason but I shall be blind.
  24. If I can check my erring love, I will;
  25. If not, to compass her I’ll use my skill.
Exit. PRO.

Milan. A street.

  1. Come on, you madcap, I’ll to the alehouse with you presently; where, for one shot of five pence, thou shalt have five thousand welcomes. But, sirrah, how did thy master part with Madam Julia?
  1. Marry, after they clos’d in earnest, they parted very fairly in jest.
  1. But shall she marry him?
  1. No.
  1. How then? Shall he marry her?
  1. No, neither.
  1. What, are they broken?
  1. No, they are both as whole as a fish.
  1. Why then, how stands the matter with them?
  1. Marry, thus: when it stands well with him, it stands well with her.
  1. What an ass art thou! I understand thee not.

  1. Ay, and what I do too. Look thee, I’ll but lean, and my staff understands me.

  1. Ask my dog. If he say ay, it will; if he say no, it will; if he shake his tail and say nothing, it will.
  1. The conclusion is then, that it will.
  1. Thou shalt never get such a secret from me but by a parable.

  1. I never knew him otherwise.

  1. A notable lubber—as thou reportest him to be.

  1. I tell thee, my master is become a hot lover.

  1. Because thou hast not so much charity in thee as to go to the ale with a Christian. Wilt thou go?

Scene 6

Milan. A room in the Duke’s palace.

Enter Proteus solus. PRO.
  1. To leave my Julia—shall I be forsworn?
  2. To love fair Silvia—shall I be forsworn?
  3. To wrong my friend, I shall be much forsworn.
  4. And ev’n that pow’r which gave me first my oath
  5. Provokes me to this threefold perjury.
  6. Love bade me swear, and Love bids me forswear.
  7. O sweet-suggesting Love, if thou hast sinn’d,
  8. Teach me, thy tempted subject, to excuse it!
  9. At first I did adore a twinkling star,
  10. But now I worship a celestial sun.
  11. Unheedful vows may heedfully be broken,
  12. And he wants wit that wants resolved will
  13. To learn his wit t’ exchange the bad for better.
  14. Fie, fie, unreverend tongue, to call her bad,
  15. Whose sovereignty so oft thou hast preferr’d
  16. With twenty thousand soul-confirming oaths.

  18. But there I leave to love where I should love.

  20. If I keep them, I needs must lose myself;
  21. If I lose them, thus find I by their loss—

  25. And Silvia (witness heaven, that made her fair)
  26. Shows Julia but a swarthy Ethiope.

  28. Rememb’ring that my love to her is dead;
  29. And Valentine I’ll hold an enemy,
  30. Aiming at Silvia as a sweeter friend.
  31. I cannot now prove constant to myself,
  32. Without some treachery us’d to Valentine.
  33. This night he meaneth with a corded ladder
  34. To climb celestial Silvia’s chamber-window,
  35. Myself in counsel his competitor.
  36. Now presently I’ll give her father notice
  37. Of their disguising and pretended flight,
  38. Who, all enrag’d, will banish Valentine;
  39. For Thurio, he intends, shall wed his daughter;
  40. But, Valentine being gone, I’ll quickly cross
  41. By some sly trick blunt Thurio’s dull proceeding.
  42. Love, lend me wings to make my purpose swift,
  43. As thou hast lent me wit to plot this drift.

Scene 7

Verona. A room in Julia’s house.

  2. And ev’n in kind love I do conjure thee,
  3. Who art the table wherein all my thoughts
  4. Are visibly character’d and engrav’d,
  5. To lesson me and tell me some good mean
  6. How with my honor I may undertake
  7. A journey to my loving Proteus.
  1. Alas, the way is wearisome and long.
  1. A true-devoted pilgrim is not weary
  2. To measure kingdoms with his feeble steps;
  3. Much less shall she that hath Love’s wings to fly,
  4. And when the flight is made to one so dear,
  5. Of such divine perfection, as Sir Proteus.
  1. Better forbear till Proteus make return.
  1. O, know’st thou not his looks are my soul’s food?
  2. Pity the dearth that I have pined in,
  3. By longing for that food so long a time.
  4. Didst thou but know the inly touch of love,
  5. Thou wouldst as soon go kindle fire with snow
  6. As seek to quench the fire of love with words.
  1. I do not seek to quench your love’s hot fire,
  2. But qualify the fire’s extreme rage,
  3. Lest it should burn above the bounds of reason.
  1. The more thou dam’st it up, the more it burns:
  2. The current that with gentle murmur glides,
  3. Thou know’st, being stopp’d, impatiently doth rage;
  4. But when his fair course is not hindered,
  5. He makes sweet music with th’ enamell’d stones,
  6. Giving a gentle kiss to every sedge
  7. He overtaketh in his pilgrimage;
  8. And so by many winding nooks he strays
  9. With willing sport to the wild ocean.
  10. Then let me go, and hinder not my course:
  11. I’ll be as patient as a gentle stream,
  12. And make a pastime of each weary step,
  13. Till the last step have brought me to my love,
  14. And there I’ll rest, as after much turmoil
  15. A blessed soul doth in Elysium.
  1. But in what habit will you go along?
  1. Not like a woman, for I would prevent
  2. The loose encounters of lascivious men:
  3. Gentle Lucetta, fit me with such weeds
  4. As may beseem some well-reputed page.
  1. Why then your ladyship must cut your hair.
  1. No, girl, I’ll knit it up in silken strings,
  2. With twenty odd-conceited true-love knots:
  3. To be fantastic may become a youth
  4. Of greater time than I shall show to be.
  1. What fashion, madam, shall I make your breeches?
  1. That fits as well as “Tell me, good my lord,
  2. What compass will you wear your farthingale?”
  3. Why, ev’n what fashion thou best likes, Lucetta.
  1. You must needs have them with a codpiece, madam.
  1. Out, out, Lucetta, that will be ill-favor’d.
  1. A round hose, madam, now’s not worth a pin,
  2. Unless you have a codpiece to stick pins on.
  1. Lucetta, as thou lov’st me, let me have
  2. What thou think’st meet, and is most mannerly.
  3. But tell me, wench, how will the world repute me
  4. For undertaking so unstaid a journey?
  5. I fear me it will make me scandaliz’d.
  1. If you think so, then stay at home and go not.
  1. Nay, that I will not.
  1. Then never dream on infamy, but go.
  2. If Proteus like your journey when you come,
  3. No matter who’s displeas’d when you are gone:
  4. I fear me he will scarce be pleas’d withal.
  1. That is the least, Lucetta, of my fear:
  2. A thousand oaths, an ocean of his tears,
  3. And instances of infinite of love,
  4. Warrant me welcome to my Proteus.
  1. All these are servants to deceitful men.
  1. Base men, that use them to so base effect!
  2. But truer stars did govern Proteus’ birth:
  3. His words are bonds, his oaths are oracles,
  4. His love sincere, his thoughts immaculate,
  5. His tears pure messengers sent from his heart,
  6. His heart as far from fraud as heaven from earth.
  1. Pray heav’n he prove so when you come to him!
  1. Now, as thou lov’st me, do him not that wrong,
  2. To bear a hard opinion of his truth:
  3. Only deserve my love by loving him,
  4. And presently go with me to my chamber,
  5. To take a note of what I stand in need of,
  6. To furnish me upon my longing journey.
  7. All that is mine I leave at thy dispose,
  8. My goods, my lands, my reputation;
  9. Only, in lieu thereof, dispatch me hence.
  10. Come; answer not; but to it presently,
  11. I am impatient of my tarriance.
Exeunt. JUL. LUC.

Act 3

Scene 1

Milan. An anteroom in the Duke’s palace.

Enter Duke, Thurio, Proteus. DUKE. PRO. THU.
  1. Sir Thurio, give us leave, I pray, a while,
  2. We have some secrets to confer about.
  3. Exit Thurio. THU.
  4. Now tell me, Proteus, what’s your will with me?
  1. My gracious lord, that which I would discover
  2. The law of friendship bids me to conceal,
  3. But when I call to mind your gracious favors
  4. Done to me (undeserving as I am),
  5. My duty pricks me on to utter that
  6. Which else no worldly good should draw from me.
  7. Know, worthy prince, Sir Valentine, my friend,
