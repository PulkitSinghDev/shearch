The First Part of Henry the Sixth

Act 1

Scene 1

Westminster Abbey.

Dead march. Enter the Funeral of King Henry the Fifth, attended on by the Duke of Bedford, the Duke of Gloucester, the Duke of Exeter, the Earl of Warwick, the Bishop of Winchester, and the Duke of Somerset; Heralds, etc. King Henry the Fifth BED. EXE. GLOU. WAR. WIN. SOM. Heralds
  1. Hung be the heavens with black, yield day to night!
  2. Comets, importing change of times and states,
  3. Brandish your crystal tresses in the sky,
  4. And with them scourge the bad revolting stars
  5. That have consented unto Henry’s death:
  6. King Henry the Fifth, too famous to live long!
  7. England ne’er lost a king of so much worth.
  1. England ne’er had a king until his time:
  2. Virtue he had, deserving to command;
  3. His brandish’d sword did blind men with his beams;
  4. His arms spread wider than a dragon’s wings;
  5. His sparkling eyes, replete with wrathful fire,
  6. More dazzled and drove back his enemies
  7. Than midday sun fierce bent against their faces.
  8. What should I say? His deeds exceed all speech:
  9. He ne’er lift up his hand but conquered.
  1. We mourn in black, why mourn we not in blood?
  2. Henry is dead, and never shall revive.
  3. Upon a wooden coffin we attend,
  4. And death’s dishonorable victory
  5. We with our stately presence glorify,
  6. Like captives bound to a triumphant car.
  7. What? Shall we curse the planets of mishap
  8. That plotted thus our glory’s overthrow?
  9. Or shall we think the subtile-witted French
  10. Conjurers and sorcerers, that, afraid of him,
  11. By magic verses have contriv’d his end?
  1. He was a king blest of the King of kings.
  2. Unto the French the dreadful Judgment Day
  3. So dreadful will not be as was his sight.
  4. The battles of the Lord of hosts he fought;
  5. The Church’s prayers made him so prosperous.
  1. The Church? Where is it? Had not churchmen pray’d,
  2. His thread of life had not so soon decay’d.
  3. None do you like but an effeminate prince,
  4. Whom like a schoolboy you may overawe.
  1. Gloucester, what e’er we like, thou art Protector,
  2. And lookest to command the Prince and realm.
  3. Thy wife is proud, she holdeth thee in awe,
  4. More than God or religious churchmen may.
  1. Name not religion, for thou lov’st the flesh,
  2. And ne’er throughout the year to church thou go’st
  3. Except it be to pray against thy foes.
  1. Cease, cease these jars and rest your minds in peace.
  2. Let’s to the altar. Heralds, wait on us.
  3. In stead of gold, we’ll offer up our arms,
  4. Since arms avail not now that Henry’s dead.
  5. Posterity, await for wretched years,
  6. When at their mothers’ moist’ned eyes babes shall suck,
  7. Our isle be made a nourish of salt tears,
  8. And none but women left to wail the dead.
  9. Henry the Fifth, thy ghost I invocate:
  10. Prosper this realm, keep it from civil broils,
  11. Combat with adverse planets in the heavens!
  12. A far more glorious star thy soul will make
  13. Than Julius Caesar or bright—
Enter a Messenger. 1. MESS.
  1. My honorable lords, health to you all!
  2. Sad tidings bring I to you out of France,
  3. Of loss, of slaughter, and discomfiture:
  4. Guienne, Champaigne, Rheims, Orléans,
  5. Paris, Guysors, Poictiers, are all quite lost.
  1. What say’st thou, man, before dead Henry’s corse?
  2. Speak softly, or the loss of those great towns
  3. Will make him burst his lead and rise from death.
  1. Is Paris lost? Is Roan yielded up?
  2. If Henry were recall’d to life again,
  3. These news would cause him once more yield the ghost.
  1. How were they lost? What treachery was us’d?
  1. No treachery, but want of men and money.
  2. Amongst the soldiers this is muttered,
  3. That here you maintain several factions;
  4. And whilst a field should be dispatch’d and fought,
  5. You are disputing of your generals.
  6. One would have ling’ring wars with little cost;
  7. Another would fly swift, but wanteth wings;
  8. A third thinks, without expense at all,
  9. By guileful fair words peace may be obtain’d.
  10. Awake, awake, English nobility!
  11. Let not sloth dim your honors new begot.
  12. Cropp’d are the flower-de-luces in your arms,
  13. Of England’s coat one half is cut away.
Exit. 1. MESS.
  1. Were our tears wanting to this funeral,
  2. These tidings would call forth her flowing tides.
  1. Me they concern, Regent I am of France.
  2. Give me my steeled coat, I’ll fight for France.
  3. Away with these disgraceful wailing robes!
  4. Wounds will I lend the French in stead of eyes,
  5. To weep their intermissive miseries.
Enter to them another Messenger. 2. MESS.
  1. Lords, view these letters full of bad mischance.
  2. France is revolted from the English quite,
  3. Except some petty towns of no import.
  4. The Dauphin Charles is crowned king in Rheims;
  5. The Bastard of Orléans with him is join’d;
  6. Reignier, Duke of Anjou, doth take his part;
  7. The Duke of Alanson flieth to his side.
Exit. 2. MESS.
  1. The Dauphin crowned king? All fly to him?
  2. O, whither shall we fly from this reproach?
  1. We will not fly but to our enemies’ throats.
  2. Bedford, if thou be slack, I’ll fight it out.
  1. Gloucester, why doubt’st thou of my forwardness?
  2. An army have I muster’d in my thoughts,
  3. Wherewith already France is overrun.
Enter another Messenger. 3. MESS.
  1. My gracious lords, to add to your laments,
  2. Wherewith you now bedew King Henry’s hearse,
  3. I must inform you of a dismal fight
  4. Betwixt the stout Lord Talbot and the French.
  1. What? Wherein Talbot overcame, is’t so?
  1. O no; wherein Lord Talbot was o’er-thrown.
  2. The circumstance I’ll tell you more at large.
  3. The tenth of August last this dreadful lord,
  4. Retiring from the siege of Orléans,
  5. Having full scarce six thousand in his troop,
  6. By three and twenty thousand of the French
  7. Was round encompassed, and set upon.
  8. No leisure had he to enrank his men;
  9. He wanted pikes to set before his archers;
  10. In stead whereof sharp stakes pluck’d out of hedges
  11. They pitched in the ground confusedly,
  12. To keep the horsemen off from breaking in.
  13. More than three hours the fight continued,
  14. Where valiant Talbot above human thought
  15. Enacted wonders with his sword and lance:
  16. Hundreds he sent to hell, and none durst stand him;
  17. Here, there, and every where, enrag’d he slew.
  18. The French exclaim’d, the devil was in arms;
  19. All the whole army stood agaz’d on him.
  20. His soldiers, spying his undaunted spirit,
  21. “A Talbot! A Talbot!” cried out amain,
  22. And rush’d into the bowels of the battle.
  23. Here had the conquest fully been seal’d up,
  24. If Sir John Falstaff had not play’d the coward.
  25. He, being in the vaward, plac’d behind
  26. With purpose to relieve and follow them,
  27. Cowardly fled, not having struck one stroke.
  28. Hence grew the general wrack and massacre;
  29. Enclosed were they with their enemies.
  30. A base Wallon, to win the Dauphin’s grace,
  31. Thrust Talbot with a spear into the back,
  32. Whom all France with their chief assembled strength
  33. Durst not presume to look once in the face.
  1. Is Talbot slain then? I will slay myself
  2. For living idly here in pomp and ease,
  3. Whilst such a worthy leader, wanting aid,
  4. Unto his dastard foemen is betray’d.
  1. O no, he lives, but is took prisoner,
  2. And Lord Scales with him, and Lord Hungerford.
  3. Most of the rest slaughter’d or took likewise.
  1. His ransom there is none but I shall pay:
  2. I’ll hale the Dauphin headlong from his throne,
  3. His crown shall be the ransom of my friend;
  4. Four of their lords I’ll change for one of ours.
  5. Farewell, my masters, to my task will I.
  6. Bonfires in France forthwith I am to make,
  7. To keep our great Saint George’s feast withal.
  8. Ten thousand soldiers with me I will take,
  9. Whose bloody deeds shall make all Europe quake.
  1. So you had need, for Orléans is besieg’d;
  2. The English army is grown weak and faint;
  3. The Earl of Salisbury craveth supply,
  4. And hardly keeps his men from mutiny,
  5. Since they, so few, watch such a multitude.
Exit. 3. MESS.
  1. Remember, lords, your oaths to Henry sworn:
  2. Either to quell the Dauphin utterly,
  3. Or bring him in obedience to your yoke.
  1. I do remember it, and here take my leave,
  2. To go about my preparation.
Exit Bedford. BED.
  1. I’ll to the Tower with all the haste I can,
  2. To view th’ artillery and munition,
  3. And then I will proclaim young Henry king.
Exit Gloucester. GLOU.
  1. To Eltam will I, where the young King is,
  2. Being ordain’d his special governor,
  3. And for his safety there I’ll best devise.
Exit. EXE.
  1. Each hath his place and function to attend:
  2. I am left out; for me nothing remains.
  3. But long I will not be Jack out of office.
  4. The King from Eltam I intend to send,
  5. And sit at chiefest stern of public weal.
Exeunt. WAR. WIN. SOM. Heralds

Scene 2

France. Before Orléans.

  1. Mars his true moving, even as in the heavens,
  2. So in the earth, to this day is not known.
  3. Late did he shine upon the English side;
  4. Now we are victors, upon us he smiles.
  5. What towns of any moment but we have?
  6. At pleasure here we lie near Orléans;
  7. Otherwhiles the famish’d English, like pale ghosts,
  8. Faintly besiege us one hour in a month.
  1. They want their porridge and their fat bull-beeves:
  2. Either they must be dieted like mules
  3. And have their provender tied to their mouths,
  4. Or piteous they will look, like drowned mice.
  1. Let’s raise the siege; why live we idly here?
  2. Talbot is taken, whom we wont to fear;
  3. Remaineth none but mad-brain’d Salisbury,
  4. And he may well in fretting spend his gall—
  5. Nor men nor money hath he to make war.
  1. Sound, sound alarum! We will rush on them.
  2. Now for the honor of the forlorn French!
  3. Him I forgive my death that killeth me,
  4. When he sees me go back one foot or fly.
Exeunt.
Here alarum; they are beaten back by the English with great loss.
Enter Charles, Alanson, and Reignier. CHAR. ALAN. REIG.
  1. Who ever saw the like? What men have I!
  2. Dogs! Cowards! Dastards! I would ne’er have fled,
  3. But that they left me midst my enemies.
  1. Salisbury is a desperate homicide,
  2. He fighteth as one weary of his life.
  3. The other lords, like lions wanting food,
  4. Do rush upon us as their hungry prey.
  1. Froissard, a countryman of ours, records
  2. England all Olivers and Rolands bred
  3. During the time Edward the Third did reign.
  4. More truly now may this be verified,
  5. For none but Samsons and Goliases
  6. It sendeth forth to skirmish. One to ten!
  7. Lean raw-bon’d rascals! Who would e’er suppose
  8. They had such courage and audacity?
  1. Let’s leave this town, for they are hare-brain’d slaves,
  2. And hunger will enforce them to be more eager.
  3. Of old I know them; rather with their teeth
  4. The walls they’ll tear down than forsake the siege.
  1. I think by some odd gimmors or device
  2. Their arms are set, like clocks, still to strike on;
  3. Else ne’er could they hold out so as they do.
  4. By my consent, we’ll even let them alone.
  1. Be it so.
Enter the Bastard of Orléans. BAST.
  1. Where’s the Prince Dauphin? I have news for him.
  1. Bastard of Orléans, thrice welcome to us.
  1. Methinks your looks are sad, your cheer appal’d.
  2. Hath the late overthrow wrought this offense?
  3. Be not dismay’d, for succor is at hand:
  4. A holy maid hither with me I bring,
  5. Which by a vision sent to her from heaven
  6. Ordained is to raise this tedious siege,
  7. And drive the English forth the bounds of France.
  8. The spirit of deep prophecy she hath,
  9. Exceeding the nine sibyls of old Rome:
  10. What’s past and what’s to come she can descry.
  11. Speak, shall I call her in? Believe my words,
  12. For they are certain and unfallible.
  1. Go call her in.
  2. Exit Bastard. BAST.
  3. But first, to try her skill,
  4. Reignier, stand thou as Dauphin in my place;
  5. Question her proudly, let thy looks be stern.
  6. By this means shall we sound what skill she hath.
Enter Joan de Pucelle and Bastard. PUC. BAST.
  1. Fair maid, is’t thou wilt do these wondrous feats?
  1. Reignier, is’t thou that thinkest to beguile me?
  2. Where is the Dauphin? Come, come from behind,
  3. I know thee well, though never seen before.
  4. Be not amaz’d, there’s nothing hid from me;
  5. In private will I talk with thee apart.
  6. Stand back, you lords, and give us leave a while.
  1. She takes upon her bravely at first dash.
  1. Dauphin, I am by birth a shepherd’s daughter,
  2. My wit untrain’d in any kind of art.
  3. Heaven and our Lady gracious hath it pleas’d
  4. To shine on my contemptible estate.
  5. Lo, whilest I waited on my tender lambs,
  6. And to sun’s parching heat display’d my cheeks,
  7. God’s Mother deigned to appear to me,
  8. And in a vision full of majesty
  9. Will’d me to leave my base vocation
  10. And free my country from calamity.
  11. Her aid she promis’d, and assur’d success;
  12. In complete glory she reveal’d herself;
  13. And whereas I was black and swart before,
  14. With those clear rays which she infus’d on me
  15. That beauty am I blest with which you may see.
  16. Ask me what question thou canst possible,
  17. And I will answer unpremeditated;
  18. My courage try by combat, if thou dar’st,
  19. And thou shalt find that I exceed my sex.
  20. Resolve on this: thou shalt be fortunate
  21. If thou receive me for thy warlike mate.
  1. Thou hast astonish’d me with thy high terms.
  2. Only this proof I’ll of thy valor make,
  3. In single combat thou shalt buckle with me;
  4. And if thou vanquishest, thy words are true,
  5. Otherwise I renounce all confidence.
  1. I am prepar’d; here is my keen-edg’d sword,
  2. Deck’d with five flower-de-luces on each side,
  3. The which at Touraine, in Saint Katherine’s church-yard,
  4. Out of a great deal of old iron I chose forth.
  1. Then come a’ God’s name, I fear no woman.
  1. And while I live, I’ll ne’er fly from a man.
Here they fight, and Joan de Pucelle overcomes. CHAR. PUC.
  1. Stay, stay thy hands! Thou art an Amazon,
  2. And fightest with the sword of Deborah.
  1. Christ’s Mother helps me, else I were too weak.
  1. Whoe’er helps thee, ’tis thou that must help me:
  2. Impatiently I burn with thy desire;
  3. My heart and hands thou hast at once subdu’d.
  4. Excellent Pucelle, if thy name be so,
  5. Let me thy servant and not sovereign be.
  6. ’Tis the French Dauphin sueth to thee thus.
  1. I must not yield to any rites of love,
  2. For my profession’s sacred from above.
  3. When I have chased all thy foes from hence,
  4. Then will I think upon a recompense.
  1. Mean time look gracious on thy prostrate thrall.
  1. My lord, methinks, is very long in talk.
  1. Doubtless he shrives this woman to her smock,
  2. Else ne’er could he so long protract his speech.
  1. Shall we disturb him, since he keeps no mean?
  1. He may mean more than we poor men do know:
  2. These women are shrewd tempters with their tongues.
  1. My lord, where are you? What devise you on?
  2. Shall we give o’er Orléans, or no?
  1. Why, no, I say. Distrustful recreants,
  2. Fight till the last gasp; I’ll be your guard.
  1. What she says I’ll confirm. We’ll fight it out.
  1. Assign’d am I to be the English scourge.
  2. This night the siege assuredly I’ll raise:
  3. Expect Saint Martin’s summer, halcyons’ days,
  4. Since I have entered into these wars.
  5. Glory is like a circle in the water,
  6. Which never ceaseth to enlarge itself,
  7. Till by broad spreading it disperse to nought.
  8. With Henry’s death the English circle ends,
  9. Dispersed are the glories it included.
  10. Now am I like that proud insulting ship
  11. Which Caesar and his fortune bare at once.
  1. Was Mahomet inspired with a dove?
  2. Thou with an eagle art inspired then.
  3. Helen, the mother of great Constantine,
  4. Nor yet Saint Philip’s daughters, were like thee.
  5. Bright star of Venus, fall’n down on the earth,
  6. How may I reverently worship thee enough?
  1. Leave off delays, and let us raise the siege.
  1. Woman, do what thou canst to save our honors;
  2. Drive them from Orléans and be immortaliz’d.
  1. Presently we’ll try; come, let’s away about it.
  2. No prophet will I trust, if she prove false.
Exeunt. CHAR. ALAN. REIG. BAST. PUC.

Scene 3

London. Before the Tower.

Enter Gloucester with his Serving Attendants in blue coats. GLOU. 1. GLOU. SERV. ATT. 2. GLOU. SERV. ATT.
  1. I am come to survey the Tower this day;
  2. Since Henry’s death, I fear, there is conveyance.
  3. Where be these warders, that they wait not here?
  4. Open the gates, ’tis Gloucester that calls.
  1. Within. 1. WARDER
  2. Who’s there, that knocks so imperiously?
  1. It is the noble Duke of Gloucester.
  1. Within. 2. WARDER
  2. Whoe’er he be, you may not be let in.
  1. Villains, answer you so the Lord Protector?
  1. Within. 1. WARDER
  2. The Lord protect him! So we answer him.
  3. We do no otherwise than we are will’d.
  1. Who willed you? Or whose will stands but mine?
  2. There’s none Protector of the realm but I.—
  3. Break up the gates, I’ll be your warrantize.
  4. Shall I be flouted thus by dunghill grooms?
Gloucester’s men rush at the Tower gates, and Woodvile the Lieutenant speaks within. 1. GLOU. SERV. ATT. 2. GLOU. SERV. ATT. WOODV.
  1. What noise is this? What traitors have we here?
  1. Lieutenant, is it you whose voice I hear?
  2. Open the gates, here’s Gloucester that would enter.
  1. Have patience, noble Duke, I may not open,
  2. The Cardinal of Winchester forbids.
  3. From him I have express commandment
  4. That thou nor none of thine shall be let in.
  1. Faint-hearted Woodvile, prizest him ’fore me?
  2. Arrogant Winchester, that haughty prelate,
  3. Whom Henry, our late sovereign, ne’er could brook?
  4. Thou art no friend to God or to the King.
  5. Open the gates, or I’ll shut thee out shortly.
  1. Open the gates unto the Lord Protector,
  2. Or we’ll burst them open, if that you come not quickly.
Enter to the Protector at the Tower gates Winchester and his Men in tawny coats. WIN.
  1. How now, ambitious Humphrey, what means this?
  1. Peel’d priest, dost thou command me to be shut out?
  1. I do, thou most usurping proditor,
  2. And not Protector, of the King or realm.
  1. Stand back, thou manifest conspirator,
  2. Thou that contrivedst to murder our dead lord,
  3. Thou that giv’st whores indulgences to sin.
  4. I’ll canvass thee in thy broad cardinal’s hat,
  5. If thou proceed in this thy insolence.
  1. Nay, stand thou back, I will not budge a foot:
  2. This be Damascus, be thou cursed Cain,
  3. To slay thy brother Abel, if thou wilt.
  1. I will not slay thee, but I’ll drive thee back.
  2. Thy scarlet robes as a child’s bearing-cloth
  3. I’ll use to carry thee out of this place.
  1. Do what thou dar’st, I beard thee to thy face.
  1. What? Am I dar’d and bearded to my face?
  2. Draw, men, for all this privileged place—
  3. Blue coats to tawny coats! Priest, beware your beard,
  4. I mean to tug it and to cuff you soundly.
  5. Under my feet I stamp thy cardinal’s hat;
  6. In spite of Pope or dignities of church,
  7. Here by the cheeks I’ll drag thee up and down.
  1. Gloucester, thou wilt answer this before the Pope.
  1. Winchester goose, I cry, “A rope! A rope!”
  2. Now beat them hence, why do you let them stay?
  3. Thee I’ll chase hence, thou wolf in sheep’s array.
  4. Out, tawny-coats! Out, scarlet hypocrite!
Here Gloucester’s men beat out the Cardinal’s men, and enter in the hurly-burly the Mayor of London and his Officers. MAY. OFF.
  1. Fie, lords, that you, being supreme magistrates,
  2. Thus contumeliously should break the peace!
  1. Peace, Mayor, thou know’st little of my wrongs.
  2. Here’s Beauford, that regards nor God nor king,
  3. Hath here distrain’d the Tower to his use.
  1. Here’s Gloucester, a foe to citizens,
  2. One that still motions war and never peace,
  3. O’ercharging your free purses with large fines;
  4. That seeks to overthrow religion
  5. Because he is Protector of the realm,
  6. And would have armor here out of the Tower,
  7. To crown himself king and suppress the Prince.
  1. I will not answer thee with words, but blows.
Here they skirmish again.
  1. Nought rests for me in this tumultuous strife
  2. But to make open proclamation.
  3. Come, officer, as loud as e’er thou canst,
  4. Cry.
  1. All manner of men assembled here in arms this day against God’s peace and the King’s, we charge and command you, in his Highness’ name, to repair to your several dwelling-places, and not to wear, handle, or use any sword, weapon, or dagger, henceforward, upon pain of death.
  1. Cardinal, I’ll be no breaker of the law;
  2. But we shall meet, and break our minds at large.
  1. Gloucester, we’ll meet to thy cost, be sure:
  2. Thy heart-blood I will have for this day’s work.
  1. I’ll call for clubs, if you will not away.
  2. This cardinal’s more haughty than the devil.
  1. Mayor, farewell; thou dost but what thou mayst.
  1. Abominable Gloucester, guard thy head,
  2. For I intend to have it ere long.
Exeunt, several ways, Gloucester and Winchester with their Servingmen. GLOU. 1. GLOU. SERV. ATT. 2. GLOU. SERV. ATT. WIN.
  1. See the coast clear’d, and then we will depart.
  2. Good God, these nobles should such stomachs bear!
  3. I myself fight not once in forty year.
Exeunt. MAY. OFF.

Scene 4

France. Before Orléans.

Enter the Master Gunner of Orléans and his Boy. M. GUN. M. GUN. BOY.
  1. Sirrah, thou know’st how Orléans is besieg’d,
  2. And how the English have the suburbs won.
  1. Father, I know, and oft have shot at them,
  2. Howe’er unfortunate I miss’d my aim.
  1. But now thou shalt not. Be thou rul’d by me.
  2. Chief master gunner am I of this town,
  3. Something I must do to procure me grace.
  4. The Prince’s espials have informed me
  5. How the English, in the suburbs close intrench’d,
  6. Wont through a secret grate of iron bars
  7. In yonder tower to overpeer the city,
  8. And thence discover how with most advantage
  9. They may vex us with shot or with assault.
  10. To intercept this inconvenience,
  11. A piece of ord’nance ’gainst it I have plac’d,
  12. And even these three days have I watch’d
  13. If I could see them.
  14. Now do thou watch, for I can stay no longer.
  15. If thou spy’st any, run and bring me word,
  16. And thou shalt find me at the Governor’s.
Exit. M. GUN.
  1. Father, I warrant you, take you no care,
  2. I’ll never trouble you, if I may spy them.
Exit. M. GUN. BOY.
Enter Salisbury and Talbot, Sir William Glansdale, Sir Thomas Gargrave, on the turrets, with others. SAL. TAL. GLAN. GAR.
  1. Talbot, my life, my joy, again return’d?
  2. How wert thou handled, being prisoner?
  3. Or by what means gots thou to be releas’d?
  4. Discourse, I prithee, on this turret’s top.
  1. The Earl of Bedford had a prisoner
  2. Call’d the brave Lord Ponton de Santrailles,
  3. For him was I exchang’d and ransomed.
  4. But with a baser man of arms by far
  5. Once in contempt they would have barter’d me;
  6. Which I, disdaining, scorn’d, and craved death
  7. Rather than I would be so pill’d esteem’d:
  8. In fine, redeem’d I was as I desir’d.
  9. But O, the treacherous Falstaff wounds my heart,
  10. Whom with my bare fists I would execute,
  11. If I now had him brought into my power.
  1. Yet tell’st thou not how thou wert entertain’d.
  1. With scoffs and scorns and contumelious taunts.
