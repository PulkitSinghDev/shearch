The Second Part of Henry the Sixth

Act 1

Scene 1

London. A room of state in the palace.

Flourish of trumpets: then hoboys. Enter King Henry, Duke Humphrey of Gloucester, Salisbury, Warwick, and Cardinal Beauford, on the one side; the Queen, Suffolk, York, Somerset, and Buckingham, on the other. KING. GLOU. SAL. WAR. CAR. QUEEN. SUF. YORK. SOM. BUCK.
  1. As by your high imperial Majesty
  2. I had in charge at my depart for France,
  3. As procurator to your Excellence,
  4. To marry Princess Margaret for your Grace;
  5. So in the famous ancient city Tours,
  6. In presence of the Kings of France and Sicil,
  7. The Dukes of Orléans, Calaber, Bretagne, and Alanson,
  8. Seven earls, twelve barons, and twenty reverend bishops,
  9. I have perform’d my task, and was espous’d;
  10. And humbly now upon my bended knee,
  11. In sight of England and her lordly peers,
  12. Deliver up my title in the Queen
  13. To your most gracious hands, that are the substance
  14. Of that great shadow I did represent:
  15. The happiest gift that ever marquess gave,
  16. The fairest queen that ever king receiv’d.
  1. Suffolk, arise. Welcome, Queen Margaret,
  2. I can express no kinder sign of love
  3. Than this kind kiss. O Lord, that lends me life,
  4. Lend me a heart replete with thankfulness!
  5. For thou hast given me in this beauteous face
  6. A world of earthly blessings to my soul,
  7. If sympathy of love unite our thoughts.
  1. Great King of England, and my gracious lord,
  2. The mutual conference that my mind hath had,
  3. By day, by night, waking and in my dreams,
  4. In courtly company, or at my beads,
  5. With you, mine alder-liefest sovereign,
  6. Makes me the bolder to salute my king
  7. With ruder terms, such as my wit affords
  8. And overjoy of heart doth minister.
  1. Her sight did ravish, but her grace in speech,
  2. Her words yclad with wisdom’s majesty,
  3. Makes me from wond’ring fall to weeping joys,
  4. Such is the fullness of my heart’s content.
  5. Lords, with one cheerful voice welcome my love.
  1. Kneel. ALL.
  2. Long live Queen Margaret, England’s happiness!
Flourish.
  1. We thank you all.
  1. My Lord Protector, so it please your Grace,
  2. Here are the articles of contracted peace
  3. Between our sovereign and the French King Charles,
  4. For eighteen months concluded by consent.
  1. Reads. GLOU.
  2. “Imprimis, It is agreed between the French King Charles, and William de la Pole, Marquess of Suffolk, ambassador for Henry King of England, that the said Henry shall espouse the Lady Margaret, daughter unto Reignier King of Naples, Sicilia, and Jerusalem, and crown her Queen of England ere the thirtieth of May next ensuing. Item, It is further agreed between them, that the duchy of Anjou and the county of Maine shall be releas’d and deliver’d over to the King her father”—
Duke Humphrey lets it fall. GLOU.
  1. Uncle, how now?
  1. Pardon me, gracious lord,
  2. Some sudden qualm hath struck me at the heart,
  3. And dimm’d mine eyes, that I can read no further.
  1. Uncle of Winchester, I pray read on.
  1. Reads. CAR.
  2. “Item, It is further agreed between them, that the duchy of Anjou and the county of Maine shall be releas’d and deliver’d over to the King her father, and she sent over of the King of England’s own proper cost and charges, without having any dowry.”
  1. They please us well. Lord Marquess, kneel down.
  2. We here create thee the first Duke of Suffolk,
  3. And girt thee with the sword. Cousin of York,
  4. We here discharge your Grace from being Regent
  5. I’ th’ parts of France, till term of eighteen months
  6. Be full expir’d. Thanks, uncle Winchester,
  7. Gloucester, York, Buckingham, Somerset,
  8. Salisbury, and Warwick;
  9. We thank you all for this great favor done
  10. In entertainment to my princely queen.
  11. Come, let us in, and with all speed provide
  12. To see her coronation be perform’d.
Exeunt King, Queen, and Suffolk. Manent the rest, stayed by Gloucester. KING. QUEEN. SUF.
  1. Brave peers of England, pillars of the state,
  2. To you Duke Humphrey must unload his grief,
  3. Your grief, the common grief of all the land.
  4. What? Did my brother Henry spend his youth,
  5. His valor, coin, and people, in the wars?
  6. Did he so often lodge in open field,
  7. In winter’s cold and summer’s parching heat,
  8. To conquer France, his true inheritance?
  9. And did my brother Bedford toil his wits,
  10. To keep by policy what Henry got?
  11. Have you yourselves, Somerset, Buckingham,
  12. Brave York, Salisbury, and victorious Warwick,
  13. Receiv’d deep scars in France and Normandy?
  14. Or hath mine uncle Beauford and myself,
  15. With all the learned Council of the realm,
  16. Studied so long, sat in the Council-house
  17. Early and late, debating to and fro
  18. How France and Frenchmen might be kept in awe,
  19. And hath his Highness in his infancy
  20. Crowned in Paris in despite of foes?
  21. And shall these labors and these honors die?
  22. Shall Henry’s conquest, Bedford’s vigilance,
  23. Your deeds of war, and all our counsel die?
  24. O peers of England, shameful is this league,
  25. Fatal this marriage, cancelling your fame,
  26. Blotting your names from books of memory,
  27. Rasing the characters of your renown,
  28. Defacing monuments of conquer’d France,
  29. Undoing all, as all had never been!
  1. Nephew, what means this passionate discourse,
  2. This peroration with such circumstance?
  3. For France, ’tis ours; and we will keep it still.
  1. Ay, uncle, we will keep it, if we can;
  2. But now it is impossible we should.
  3. Suffolk, the new-made duke that rules the roast,
  4. Hath given the duchy of Anjou, and Maine,
  5. Unto the poor King Reignier, whose large style
  6. Agrees not with the leanness of his purse.
  1. Now by the death of Him that died for all,
  2. These counties were the keys of Normandy.
  3. But wherefore weeps Warwick, my valiant son?
  1. For grief that they are past recovery;
  2. For were there hope to conquer them again,
  3. My sword should shed hot blood, mine eyes no tears.
  4. Anjou and Maine? Myself did win them both.
  5. Those provinces these arms of mine did conquer,
  6. And are the cities that I got with wounds
  7. Deliver’d up again with peaceful words?
  8. Mort Dieu!
  1. For Suffolk’s duke, may he be suffocate,
  2. That dims the honor of this warlike isle!
  3. France should have torn and rent my very heart
  4. Before I would have yielded to this league.
  5. I never read but England’s kings have had
  6. Large sums of gold and dowries with their wives,
  7. And our King Henry gives away his own,
  8. To match with her that brings no vantages.
  1. A proper jest, and never heard before,
  2. That Suffolk should demand a whole fifteenth
  3. For costs and charges in transporting her!
  4. She should have stay’d in France, and starv’d in France,
  5. Before—
  1. My Lord of Gloucester, now ye grow too hot:
  2. It was the pleasure of my lord the King.
  1. My Lord of Winchester, I know your mind.
  2. ’Tis not my speeches that you do mislike,
  3. But ’tis my presence that doth trouble ye;
  4. Rancor will out. Proud prelate, in thy face
  5. I see thy fury. If I longer stay,
  6. We shall begin our ancient bickerings.
  7. Lordings, farewell, and say, when I am gone,
  8. I prophesied France will be lost ere long.
Exit Humphrey. GLOU.
  1. So, there goes our Protector in a rage.
  2. ’Tis known to you he is mine enemy;
  3. Nay more, an enemy unto you all,
  4. And no great friend, I fear me, to the King.
  5. Consider, lords, he is the next of blood,
  6. And heir-apparent to the English crown.
  7. Had Henry got an empire by his marriage,
  8. And all the wealthy kingdoms of the west,
  9. There’s reason he should be displeas’d at it.
  10. Look to it, lords, let not his smoothing words
  11. Bewitch your hearts. Be wise and circumspect.
  12. What though the common people favor him,
  13. Calling him “Humphrey, the good Duke of Gloucester,”
  14. Clapping their hands, and crying with loud voice,
  15. “Jesu maintain your royal Excellence!”
  16. With “God preserve the good Duke Humphrey!”
  17. I fear me, lords, for all this flattering gloss,
  18. He will be found a dangerous Protector.
  1. Why should he then protect our sovereign,
  2. He being of age to govern of himself?
  3. Cousin of Somerset, join you with me,
  4. And all together, with the Duke of Suffolk,
  5. We’ll quickly hoise Duke Humphrey from his seat.
  1. This weighty business will not brook delay,
  2. I’ll to the Duke of Suffolk presently.
Exit Cardinal. CAR.
  1. Cousin of Buckingham, though Humphrey’s pride
  2. And greatness of his place be grief to us,
  3. Yet let us watch the haughty Cardinal;
  4. His insolence is more intolerable
  5. Than all the princes in the land beside.
  6. If Gloucester be displac’d, he’ll be Protector.
  1. Or thou or I, Somerset, will be Protector,
  2. Despite Duke Humphrey or the Cardinal.
Exeunt Buckingham and Somerset. BUCK. SOM.
  1. Pride went before, ambition follows him.
  2. While these do labor for their own preferment,
  3. Behooves it us to labor for the realm.
  4. I never saw but Humphrey Duke of Gloucester
  5. Did bear him like a noble gentleman.
  6. Oft have I seen the haughty Cardinal,
  7. More like a soldier than a man o’ th’ church,
  8. As stout and proud as he were lord of all,
  9. Swear like a ruffian, and demean himself
  10. Unlike the ruler of a commonweal.
  11. Warwick, my son, the comfort of my age,
  12. Thy deeds, thy plainness, and thy house-keeping,
  13. Hath won the greatest favor of the commons,
  14. Excepting none but good Duke Humphrey;
  15. And, brother York, thy acts in Ireland,
  16. In bringing them to civil discipline,
  17. Thy late exploits done in the heart of France
  18. When thou wert Regent for our sovereign,
  19. Have made thee fear’d and honor’d of the people;
  20. Join we together, for the public good,
  21. In what we can to bridle and suppress
  22. The pride of Suffolk and the Cardinal,
  23. With Somerset’s and Buckingham’s ambition;
  24. And as we may, cherish Duke Humphrey’s deeds
  25. While they do tend the profit of the land.
  1. So God help Warwick, as he loves the land
  2. And common profit of his country!
  1. And so says York—
  2. Aside. YORK.
  3. for he hath greatest cause.
  1. Then let’s make haste away, and look unto the main.
  1. Unto the main? O father, Maine is lost!
  2. That Maine which by main force Warwick did win,
  3. And would have kept so long as breath did last!
  4. Main chance, father, you meant, but I meant Maine,
  5. Which I will win from France, or else be slain.
Exeunt Warwick and Salisbury. Manet York. WAR. SAL.
  1. Anjou and Maine are given to the French,
  2. Paris is lost, the state of Normandy
  3. Stands on a tickle point now they are gone.
  4. Suffolk concluded on the articles,
  5. The peers agreed, and Henry was well pleas’d
  6. To change two dukedoms for a duke’s fair daughter.
  7. I cannot blame them all, what is’t to them?
  8. ’Tis thine they give away, and not their own.
  9. Pirates may make cheap pennyworths of their pillage
  10. And purchase friends and give to courtezans,
  11. Still reveling like lords till all be gone;
  12. While as the silly owner of the goods
  13. Weeps over them, and wrings his hapless hands,
  14. And shakes his head, and trembling stands aloof,
  15. While all is shar’d and all is borne away,
  16. Ready to starve, and dare not touch his own.
  17. So York must sit, and fret, and bite his tongue,
  18. While his own lands are bargain’d for and sold.
  19. Methinks the realms of England, France, and Ireland
  20. Bear that proportion to my flesh and blood
  21. As did the fatal brand Althaea burnt
  22. Unto the Prince’s heart of Calydon.
  23. Anjou and Maine both given unto the French!
  24. Cold news for me; for I had hope of France,
  25. Even as I have of fertile England’s soil.
  26. A day will come when York shall claim his own,
  27. And therefore I will take the Nevils’ parts,
  28. And make a show of love to proud Duke Humphrey,
  29. And, when I spy advantage, claim the crown,
  30. For that’s the golden mark I seek to hit.
  31. Nor shall proud Lancaster usurp my right,
  32. Nor hold the sceptre in his childish fist,
  33. Nor wear the diadem upon his head,
  34. Whose church-like humors fits not for a crown.
  35. Then, York, be still awhile, till time do serve.
  36. Watch thou, and wake when others be asleep,
  37. To pry into the secrets of the state,
  38. Till Henry, surfeiting in joys of love
  39. With his new bride and England’s dear-bought queen,
  40. And Humphrey with the peers be fall’n at jars:
  41. Then will I raise aloft the milk-white rose,
  42. With whose sweet smell the air shall be perfum’d,
  43. And in my standard bear the arms of York,
  44. To grapple with the house of Lancaster;
  45. And force perforce I’ll make him yield the crown,
  46. Whose bookish rule hath pull’d fair England down.
Exit York. YORK.

Scene 2

London. A room in Gloucester’s house.

Enter Duke Humphrey of Gloucester and his wife Eleanor the Duchess. GLOU. DUCH.
  1. Why droops my lord, like over-ripen’d corn
  2. Hanging the head at Ceres’ plenteous load?
  3. Why doth the great Duke Humphrey knit his brows,
  4. As frowning at the favors of the world?
  5. Why are thine eyes fix’d to the sullen earth,
  6. Gazing on that which seems to dim thy sight?
  7. What seest thou there? King Henry’s diadem,
  8. Enchas’d with all the honors of the world?
  9. If so, gaze on, and grovel on thy face,
  10. Until thy head be circled with the same.
  11. Put forth thy hand, reach at the glorious gold.
  12. What, is’t too short? I’ll lengthen it with mine,
  13. And having both together heav’d it up,
  14. We’ll both together lift our heads to heaven,
  15. And never more abase our sight so low
  16. As to vouchsafe one glance unto the ground.
  1. O Nell, sweet Nell, if thou dost love thy lord,
  2. Banish the canker of ambitious thoughts!
  3. And may that thought, when I imagine ill
  4. Against my king and nephew, virtuous Henry,
  5. Be my last breathing in this mortal world!
  6. My troublous dreams this night doth make me sad.
  1. What dream’d my lord? Tell me, and I’ll requite it
  2. With sweet rehearsal of my morning’s dream.
  1. Methought this staff, mine office-badge in court,
  2. Was broke in twain (by whom I have forgot,
  3. But, as I think, it was by th’ Cardinal),
  4. And on the pieces of the broken wand
  5. Were plac’d the heads of Edmund Duke of Somerset,
  6. And William de la Pole, first Duke of Suffolk.
  7. This was my dream, what it doth bode God knows.
  1. Tut, this was nothing but an argument
  2. That he that breaks a stick of Gloucester’s grove
  3. Shall lose his head for his presumption.
  4. But list to me, my Humphrey, my sweet duke:
  5. Methought I sate in seat of majesty
  6. In the cathedral church of Westminster,
  7. And in that chair where kings and queens were crown’d,
  8. Where Henry and Dame Margaret kneel’d to me,
  9. And on my head did set the diadem.
  1. Nay, Eleanor, then must I chide outright.
  2. Presumptuous dame, ill-nurtur’d Eleanor,
  3. Art thou not second woman in the realm?
  4. And the Protector’s wife, belov’d of him?
  5. Hast thou not worldly pleasure at command
  6. Above the reach or compass of thy thought?
  7. And wilt thou still be hammering treachery,
  8. To tumble down thy husband and thyself
  9. From top of honor to disgrace’s feet?
  10. Away from me, and let me hear no more!
  1. What, what, my lord? Are you so choleric
  2. With Eleanor, for telling but her dream?
  3. Next time I’ll keep my dreams unto myself,
  4. And not be check’d.
  1. Nay, be not angry, I am pleas’d again.
Enter Messenger. 1. ROYAL MESS.
  1. My Lord Protector, ’tis his Highness’ pleasure
  2. You do prepare to ride unto Saint Albans,
  3. Where as the King and Queen do mean to hawk.
  1. I go. Come, Nell, thou wilt ride with us?
  1. Yes, my good lord, I’ll follow presently.
  2. Exit Humphrey with Messenger. GLOU. 1. ROYAL MESS.
  3. Follow I must, I cannot go before
  4. While Gloucester bears this base and humble mind.
  5. Were I a man, a duke, and next of blood,
  6. I would remove these tedious stumbling-blocks,
  7. And smooth my way upon their headless necks;
  8. And, being a woman, I will not be slack
  9. To play my part in Fortune’s pageant.
  10. Where are you there? Sir John! Nay, fear not, man,
  11. We are alone, here’s none but thee and I.
Enter Hume. HUME.
  1. Jesus preserve your royal Majesty!
  1. What say’st thou? Majesty? I am but Grace.
  1. But, by the grace of God and Hume’s advice,
  2. Your Grace’s title shall be multiplied.
  1. What say’st thou, man? Hast thou as yet conferr’d
  2. With Margery Jourdain, the cunning witch,
  3. With Roger Bolingbrook, the conjurer?
  4. And will they undertake to do me good?
  1. This they have promised, to show your Highness
  2. A spirit rais’d from depth of under ground,
  3. That shall make answer to such questions
  4. As by your Grace shall be propounded him.
  1. It is enough, I’ll think upon the questions.
  2. When from Saint Albans we do make return,
  3. We’ll see these things effected to the full.
  4. Here, Hume, take this reward. Make merry, man,
  5. With thy confederates in this weighty cause.
Exit Eleanor. DUCH.
  1. Hume must make merry with the Duchess’ gold;
  2. Marry, and shall. But how now, Sir John Hume?
  3. Seal up your lips, and give no words but mum;
  4. The business asketh silent secrecy.
  5. Dame Eleanor gives gold to bring the witch;
  6. Gold cannot come amiss, were she a devil.
  7. Yet have I gold flies from another coast—
  8. I dare not say from the rich Cardinal
  9. And from the great and new-made Duke of Suffolk;
  10. Yet I do find it so; for, to be plain,
  11. They, knowing Dame Eleanor’s aspiring humor,
  12. Have hired me to undermine the Duchess,
  13. And buzz these conjurations in her brain.
  14. They say, “A crafty knave does need no broker,”
  15. Yet am I Suffolk and the Cardinal’s broker.
  16. Hume, if you take not heed, you shall go near
  17. To call them both a pair of crafty knaves.
  18. Well, so it stands; and thus, I fear, at last
  19. Hume’s knavery will be the Duchess’ wrack,
  20. And her attainture will be Humphrey’s fall.
  21. Sort how it will, I shall have gold for all.
Exit. HUME.

Scene 3

London. A room in the palace.

Enter three or four Petitioners, Peter the Armorer’s man being one. 1. PETIT. 2. PETIT. PETER.
  1. My masters, let’s stand close. My Lord Protector will come this way by and by, and then we may deliver our supplications in the quill.
  1. Marry, the Lord protect him, for he’s a good man! Jesu bless him!
Enter Suffolk and Queen. SUF. QUEEN.
  1. Here ’a comes, methinks, and the Queen with him. I’ll be the first, sure.
  1. Come back, fool. This is the Duke of Suffolk and not my Lord Protector.
  1. How now, fellow? Wouldst any thing with me?
  1. I pray, my lord, pardon me, I took ye for my Lord Protector.
  1. Reading. QUEEN.
  2. “To my Lord Protector”?
  3. Are your supplications to his lordship? Let me see them. What is thine?
  1. Mine is, and’t please your Grace, against John Goodman, my Lord Cardinal’s man, for keeping my house, and lands, and wife and all, from me.
  1. Thy wife too? That’s some wrong indeed. What’s yours? What’s here?
  2. Reads. SUF.
  3. “Against the Duke of Suffolk, for enclosing the commons of Melford.” How now, sir knave?
  1. Alas, sir, I am but a poor petitioner of our whole township.
  1. Giving his petition. PETER.
  2. Against my master, Thomas Horner, for saying that the Duke of York was rightful heir to the crown.
  1. What say’st thou? Did the Duke of York say he was rightful heir to the crown?
  1. That my master was? No, forsooth; my master said that he was, and that the King was an usurper.
  1. Who is there?
  2. Enter Servant. Servant
  3. Take this fellow in, and send for his master with a pursuivant presently. We’ll hear more of your matter before the King.
Exit Servant with Peter. Servant PETER.
  1. And as for you, that love to be protected
  2. Under the wings of our Protector’s grace,
  3. Begin your suits anew, and sue to him.
  4. Tear the supplication. QUEEN.
  5. Away, base cullions! Suffolk, let them go.
  1. Come, let’s be gone.
Exeunt. 1. PETIT. 2. PETIT.
  1. My Lord of Suffolk, say, is this the guise,
  2. Is this the fashions in the court of England?
  3. Is this the government of Britain’s isle,
  4. And this the royalty of Albion’s king?
  5. What, shall King Henry be a pupil still
  6. Under the surly Gloucester’s governance?
  7. Am I a queen in title and in style,
  8. And must be made a subject to a duke?
  9. I tell thee, Pole, when in the city Tours
  10. Thou ran’st a-tilt in honor of my love
  11. And stol’st away the ladies’ hearts of France,
  12. I thought King Henry had resembled thee
  13. In courage, courtship, and proportion;
  14. But all his mind is bent to holiness,
  15. To number Ave-Maries on his beads;
  16. His champions are the prophets and apostles,
  17. His weapons holy saws of sacred writ,
  18. His study is his tilt-yard, and his loves
  19. Are brazen images of canonized saints.
  20. I would the college of the Cardinals
  21. Would choose him Pope and carry him to Rome,
  22. And set the triple crown upon his head—
  23. That were a state fit for his holiness.
  1. Madam, be patient. As I was cause
  2. Your Highness came to England, so will I
  3. In England work your Grace’s full content.
  1. Beside the haughty Protector, have we Beauford
  2. The imperious churchman, Somerset, Buckingham,
  3. And grumbling York; and not the least of these
  4. But can do more in England than the King.
  1. And he of these that can do most of all
  2. Cannot do more in England than the Nevils:
  3. Salisbury and Warwick are no simple peers.
  1. Not all these lords do vex me half so much
  2. As that proud dame, the Lord Protector’s wife:
  3. She sweeps it through the court with troops of ladies,
  4. More like an empress than Duke Humphrey’s wife.
  5. Strangers in court do take her for the Queen.
  6. She bears a duke’s revenues on her back,
  7. And in her heart she scorns our poverty.
  8. Shall I not live to be aveng’d on her?
  9. Contemptuous base-born callot as she is,
  10. She vaunted ’mongst her minions t’ other day,
  11. The very train of her worst wearing gown
  12. Was better worth than all my father’s lands,
  13. Till Suffolk gave two dukedoms for his daughter.
  1. Madam, myself have lim’d a bush for her,
  2. And plac’d a choir of such enticing birds
  3. That she will light to listen to the lays,
  4. And never mount to trouble you again.
  5. So let her rest; and, madam, list to me,
  6. For I am bold to counsel you in this.
  7. Although we fancy not the Cardinal,
  8. Yet must we join with him and with the lords,
  9. Till we have brought Duke Humphrey in disgrace.
  10. As for the Duke of York, this late complaint
  11. Will make but little for his benefit.
  12. So one by one we’ll weed them all at last,
  13. And you yourself shall steer the happy helm.
Sound a sennet. Enter the King, Duke Humphrey of Gloucester, Cardinal, Buckingham, York, Somerset, Salisbury, Warwick, and the Duchess of Gloucester. KING. GLOU. CAR. BUCK. YORK. SOM. SAL. WAR. DUCH.
  1. For my part, noble lords, I care not which,
  2. Or Somerset or York, all’s one to me.
  1. If York have ill demean’d himself in France,
  2. Then let him be denay’d the regentship.
  1. If Somerset be unworthy of the place,
  2. Let York be Regent, I will yield to him.
  1. Whether your Grace be worthy, yea or no,
  2. Dispute not that; York is the worthier.
  1. Ambitious Warwick, let thy betters speak.
  1. The Cardinal’s not my better in the field.
  1. All in this presence are thy betters, Warwick.
  1. Warwick may live to be the best of all.
  1. Peace, son, and show some reason, Buckingham,
  2. Why Somerset should be preferr’d in this.
  1. Because the King, forsooth, will have it so.
  1. Madam, the King is old enough himself
  2. To give his censure. These are no women’s matters.
  1. If he be old enough, what needs your Grace
  2. To be Protector of his Excellence?
  1. Madam, I am Protector of the realm,
  2. And at his pleasure will resign my place.
  1. Resign it then and leave thine insolence.
  2. Since thou wert king—as who is king but thou?—
