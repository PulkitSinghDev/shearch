The Comedy of Errors

Act 1

Scene 1

A hall in Duke Solinus’s Palace.

Enter the Duke of Ephesus with Egeon the merchant of Syracuse, Jailer with Officers, and other Attendants. DUKE. EGE. JAIL. Attendants
  1. Proceed, Solinus, to procure my fall,
  2. And by the doom of death end woes and all.
  1. Merchant of Syracuse, plead no more.
  2. I am not partial to infringe our laws;
  3. The enmity and discord which of late
  4. Sprung from the rancorous outrage of your Duke
  5. To merchants, our well-dealing countrymen,
  6. Who, wanting guilders to redeem their lives,
  7. Have seal’d his rigorous statutes with their bloods,
  8. Excludes all pity from our threat’ning looks:
  9. For since the mortal and intestine jars
  10. ’Twixt thy seditious countrymen and us,
  11. It hath in solemn synods been decreed,
  12. Both by the Syracusians and ourselves,
  13. To admit no traffic to our adverse towns:
  14. Nay more, if any born at Ephesus be seen
  15. At any Syracusian marts and fairs;
  16. Again, if any Syracusian born
  17. Come to the bay of Ephesus, he dies,
  18. His goods confiscate to the Duke’s dispose,
  19. Unless a thousand marks be levied
  20. To quit the penalty and to ransom him.
  21. Thy substance, valued at the highest rate,
  22. Cannot amount unto a hundred marks,
  23. Therefore by law thou art condemn’d to die.
  1. Yet this my comfort, when your words are done,
  2. My woes end likewise with the evening sun.
  1. Well, Syracusian; say in brief the cause
  2. Why thou departedst from thy native home,
  3. And for what cause thou cam’st to Ephesus.
  1. A heavier task could not have been impos’d
  2. Than I to speak my griefs unspeakable:
  3. Yet that the world may witness that my end
  4. Was wrought by nature, not by vile offense,
  5. I’ll utter what my sorrow gives me leave.
  6. In Syracuse was I born, and wed
  7. Unto a woman, happy but for me,
  8. And by me, had not our hap been bad:
  9. With her I liv’d in joy; our wealth increas’d
  10. By prosperous voyages I often made
  11. To Epidamium, till my factor’s death,
  12. And the great care of goods at random left,
  13. Drew me from kind embracements of my spouse;
  14. From whom my absence was not six months old
  15. Before herself (almost at fainting under
  16. The pleasing punishment that women bear)
  17. Had made provision for her following me,
  18. And soon, and safe, arrived where I was.
  19. There had she not been long but she became
  20. A joyful mother of two goodly sons:
  21. And, which was strange, the one so like the other
  22. As could not be distinguish’d but by names.
  23. That very hour, and in the self-same inn,
  24. A mean woman was delivered
  25. Of such a burden male, twins both alike.
  26. Those, for their parents were exceeding poor,
  27. I bought, and brought up to attend my sons.
  28. My wife, not meanly proud of two such boys,
  29. Made daily motions for our home return:
  30. Unwilling I agreed. Alas! Too soon
  31. We came aboard.
  32. A league from Epidamium had we sail’d
  33. Before the always-wind-obeying deep
  34. Gave any tragic instance of our harm:
  35. But longer did we not retain much hope;
  36. For what obscured light the heavens did grant
  37. Did but convey unto our fearful minds
  38. A doubtful warrant of immediate death,
  39. Which though myself would gladly have embrac’d,
  40. Yet the incessant weepings of my wife,
  41. Weeping before for what she saw must come,
  42. And piteous plainings of the pretty babes,
  43. That mourn’d for fashion, ignorant what to fear,
  44. Forc’d me to seek delays for them and me.
  45. And this it was (for other means was none):
  46. The sailors sought for safety by our boat,
  47. And left the ship, then sinking-ripe, to us.
  48. My wife, more careful for the latter-born,
  49. Had fast’ned him unto a small spare mast,
  50. Such as sea-faring men provide for storms;
  51. To him one of the other twins was bound,
  52. Whilst I had been like heedful of the other.
  53. The children thus dispos’d, my wife and I,
  54. Fixing our eyes on whom our care was fix’d,
  55. Fast’ned ourselves at either end the mast,
  56. And floating straight, obedient to the stream,
  57. Was carried towards Corinth, as we thought.
  58. At length the sun, gazing upon the earth,
  59. Dispers’d those vapors that offended us,
  60. And by the benefit of his wished light
  61. The seas wax’d calm, and we discovered
  62. Two ships from far, making amain to us,
  63. Of Corinth that, of Epidaurus this.
  64. But ere they came—O, let me say no more!
  65. Gather the sequel by that went before.
  1. Nay, forward, old man, do not break off so,
  2. For we may pity, though not pardon thee.
  1. O, had the gods done so, I had not now
  2. Worthily term’d them merciless to us!
  3. For ere the ships could meet by twice five leagues,
  4. We were encount’red by a mighty rock,
  5. Which being violently borne upon,
  6. Our helpful ship was splitted in the midst;
  7. So that, in this unjust divorce of us,
  8. Fortune had left to both of us alike
  9. What to delight in, what to sorrow for.
  10. Her part, poor soul! Seeming as burdened
  11. With lesser weight, but not with lesser woe,
  12. Was carried with more speed before the wind,
  13. And in our sight they three were taken up
  14. By fishermen of Corinth, as we thought.
  15. At length, another ship had seiz’d on us,
  16. And knowing whom it was their hap to save,
  17. Gave healthful welcome to their shipwrack’d guests,
  18. And would have reft the fishers of their prey,
  19. Had not their bark been very slow of sail;
  20. And therefore homeward did they bend their course.
  21. Thus have you heard me sever’d from my bliss,
  22. That by misfortunes was my life prolong’d,
  23. To tell sad stories of my own mishaps.
  1. And for the sake of them thou sorrowest for,
  2. Do me the favor to dilate at full
  3. What have befall’n of them and thee till now.
  1. My youngest boy, and yet my eldest care,
  2. At eighteen years became inquisitive
  3. After his brother; and importun’d me
  4. That his attendant—so his case was like,
  5. Reft of his brother, but retain’d his name—
  6. Might bear him company in the quest of him:
  7. Whom whilst I labored of a love to see,
  8. I hazarded the loss of whom I lov’d.
  9. Five summers have I spent in farthest Greece,
  10. Roaming clean through the bounds of Asia,
  11. And coasting homeward, came to Ephesus;
  12. Hopeless to find, yet loath to leave unsought
  13. Or that, or any place that harbors men.
  14. But here must end the story of my life,
  15. And happy were I in my timely death,
  16. Could all my travels warrant me they live.
  1. Hapless Egeon, whom the fates have mark’d
  2. To bear the extremity of dire mishap!
  3. Now trust me, were it not against our laws,
  4. Against my crown, my oath, my dignity,
  5. Which princes, would they, may not disannul,
  6. My soul should sue as advocate for thee:
  7. But though thou art adjudged to the death,
  8. And passed sentence may not be recall’d
  9. But to our honor’s great disparagement,
  10. Yet will I favor thee in what I can;
  11. Therefore, merchant, I’ll limit thee this day
  12. To seek thy health by beneficial help.
  13. Try all the friends thou hast in Ephesus;
  14. Beg thou, or borrow, to make up the sum,
  15. And live: if no, then thou art doom’d to die.
  16. Jailer, take him to thy custody.
  1. I will, my lord.
  1. Hopeless and helpless doth Egeon wend,
  2. But to procrastinate his lifeless end.
Exeunt. DUKE. EGE. JAIL. Attendants

Scene 2

The mart.

Enter Antipholus Erotes of Syracuse, First Merchant, and Dromio of Syracuse. S. ANT. 1. MER. S. DRO.
  1. Therefore give out you are of Epidamium,
  2. Lest that your goods too soon be confiscate:
  3. This very day a Syracusian merchant
  4. Is apprehended for arrival here;
  5. And not being able to buy out his life,
  6. According to the statute of the town,
  7. Dies ere the weary sun set in the west.
  8. There is your money that I had to keep.
  1. Go bear it to the Centaur, where we host,
  2. And stay there, Dromio, till I come to thee.
  3. Within this hour it will be dinner-time;
  4. Till that, I’ll view the manners of the town,
  5. Peruse the traders, gaze upon the buildings,
  6. And then return and sleep within mine inn,
  7. For with long travel I am stiff and weary.
  8. Get thee away.
  1. Many a man would take you at your word,
  2. And go indeed, having so good a mean.
Exit Dromio. S. DRO.
  1. A trusty villain, sir, that very oft,
  2. When I am dull with care and melancholy,
  3. Lightens my humor with his merry jests.
  4. What, will you walk with me about the town,
  5. And then go to my inn and dine with me?
  1. I am invited, sir, to certain merchants,
  2. Of whom I hope to make much benefit;
  3. I crave your pardon. Soon at five a’ clock,
  4. Please you, I’ll meet with you upon the mart,
  5. And afterward consort you till bed-time:
  6. My present business calls me from you now.
  1. Farewell till then. I will go lose myself,
  2. And wander up and down to view the city.
  1. Sir, I commend you to your own content.
Exit. 1. MER.
  1. He that commends me to mine own content,
  2. Commends me to the thing I cannot get:
  3. I to the world am like a drop of water,
  4. That in the ocean seeks another drop,
  5. Who, falling there to find his fellow forth
  6. (Unseen, inquisitive), confounds himself.
  7. So I, to find a mother and a brother,
  8. In quest of them (unhappy), ah, lose myself.
  9. Enter Dromio of Ephesus. E. DRO.
  10. Here comes the almanac of my true date.
  11. What now? How chance thou art return’d so soon?
  1. Return’d so soon! Rather approach’d too late:
  2. The capon burns, the pig falls from the spit;
  3. The clock hath strucken twelve upon the bell:
  4. My mistress made it one upon my cheek:
  5. She is so hot, because the meat is cold:
  6. The meat is cold, because you come not home:
  7. You come not home, because you have no stomach:
  8. You have no stomach, having broke your fast:
  9. But we that know what ’tis to fast and pray,
  10. Are penitent for your default today.
  1. Stop in your wind, sir; tell me this, I pray:
  2. Where have you left the money that I gave you?
  1. O—sixpence that I had a’ We’n’sday last
  2. To pay the saddler for my mistress’ crupper?
  3. The saddler had it, sir, I kept it not.
  1. I am not in a sportive humor now:
  2. Tell me, and dally not, where is the money?
  3. We being strangers here, how dar’st thou trust
  4. So great a charge from thine own custody?
  1. I pray you jest, sir, as you sit at dinner.
  2. I from my mistress come to you in post:
  3. If I return, I shall be post indeed,
  4. For she will score your fault upon my pate:
  5. Methinks your maw, like mine, should be your clock,
  6. And strike you home without a messenger.
  1. Come, Dromio, come, these jests are out of season,
  2. Reserve them till a merrier hour than this:
  3. Where is the gold I gave in charge to thee?
  1. To me, sir? Why, you gave no gold to me.
  1. Come on, sir knave, have done your foolishness,
  2. And tell me how thou hast dispos’d thy charge.
  1. My charge was but to fetch you from the mart
  2. Home to your house, the Phoenix, sir, to dinner;
  3. My mistress and her sister stays for you.
  1. Now, as I am a Christian, answer me,
  2. In what safe place you have bestow’d my money;
  3. Or I shall break that merry sconce of yours
  4. That stands on tricks when I am undispos’d:
  5. Where is the thousand marks thou hadst of me?
  1. I have some marks of yours upon my pate;
  2. Some of my mistress’ marks upon my shoulders;
  3. But not a thousand marks between you both.
  4. If I should pay your worship those again,
  5. Perchance you will not bear them patiently.
  1. Thy mistress’ marks? What mistress, slave, hast thou?
  1. Your worship’s wife, my mistress at the Phoenix;
  2. She that doth fast till you come home to dinner;
  3. And prays that you will hie you home to dinner.
  1. What, wilt thou flout me thus unto my face,
  2. Being forbid? There, take you that, sir knave.
Strikes Dromio. S. ANT. E. DRO.
  1. What mean you, sir? For God sake hold your hands!
  2. Nay, and you will not, sir, I’ll take my heels.
Exit Dromio of Ephesus. E. DRO.
  1. Upon my life, by some device or other
  2. The villain is o’erraught of all my money.
  3. They say this town is full of cozenage:
  4. As nimble jugglers that deceive the eye,
  5. Dark-working sorcerers that change the mind,
  6. Soul-killing witches that deform the body,
  7. Disguised cheaters, prating mountebanks,
  8. And many such-like liberties of sin:
  9. If it prove so, I will be gone the sooner.
  10. I’ll to the Centaur to go seek this slave;
  11. I greatly fear my money is not safe.
Exit. S. ANT.

Act 2

Scene 1

The house of Antipholus of Ephesus.

Enter Adriana, wife to Antipholus Sereptus of Ephesus, with Luciana, her sister. ADR. LUC.
  1. Neither my husband nor the slave return’d,
  2. That in such haste I sent to seek his master?
  3. Sure, Luciana, it is two a’ clock.
  1. Perhaps some merchant hath invited him,
  2. And from the mart he’s somewhere gone to dinner.
  3. Good sister, let us dine, and never fret;
  4. A man is master of his liberty:
  5. Time is their master, and when they see time,
  6. They’ll go or come; if so, be patient, sister.
  1. Why should their liberty than ours be more?
  1. Because their business still lies out a’ door.
  1. Look when I serve him so, he takes it ill.
  1. O, know he is the bridle of your will.
  1. There’s none but asses will be bridled so.
  1. Why, headstrong liberty is lash’d with woe:
  2. There’s nothing situate under heaven’s eye
  3. But hath his bound in earth, in sea, in sky.
  4. The beasts, the fishes, and the winged fowls
  5. Are their males’ subjects and at their controls:
  6. Man, more divine, the master of all these,
  7. Lord of the wide world and wild wat’ry seas,
  8. Indu’d with intellectual sense and souls,
  9. Of more pre-eminence than fish and fowls,
  10. Are masters to their females, and their lords:
  11. Then let your will attend on their accords.
  1. This servitude makes you to keep unwed.
  1. Not this, but troubles of the marriage-bed.
  1. But, were you wedded, you would bear some sway.
  1. Ere I learn love, I’ll practice to obey.
  1. How if your husband start some other where?
  1. Till he come home again, I would forbear.
  1. Patience unmov’d! No marvel though she pause—
  2. They can be meek that have no other cause:
  3. A wretched soul, bruis’d with adversity,
  4. We bid be quiet when we hear it cry;
  5. But were we burd’ned with like weight of pain,
  6. As much, or more, we should ourselves complain:
  7. So thou, that hast no unkind mate to grieve thee,
  8. With urging helpless patience would relieve me;
  9. But if thou live to see like right bereft,
  10. This fool-begg’d patience in thee will be left.
  1. Well, I will marry one day, but to try.
  2. Here comes your man, now is your husband nigh.
Enter Dromio of Ephesus. E. DRO.
  1. Say, is your tardy master now at hand?
  1. Nay, he’s at two hands with me, and that my two ears can witness.
  1. Say, didst thou speak with him? Know’st thou his mind?
  1. Ay, ay, he told his mind upon mine ear. Beshrew his hand, I scarce could understand it.
  1. Spake he so doubtfully, thou couldst not feel his meaning?
  1. Nay, he struck so plainly, I could too well feel his blows; and withal so doubtfully, that I could scarce understand them.
  1. But say, I prithee, is he coming home?
  2. It seems he hath great care to please his wife.
  1. Why, mistress, sure my master is horn-mad.
  1. Horn-mad, thou villain!
  1. I mean not cuckold-mad—
  2. But sure he is stark mad:
  3. When I desir’d him to come home to dinner,
  4. He ask’d me for a thousand marks in gold:
  5. “’Tis dinner-time,” quoth I: “My gold!” quoth he.
  6. “Your meat doth burn,” quoth I: “My gold!” quoth he.
  7. “Will you come?” quoth I: “My gold!” quoth he;
  8. “Where is the thousand marks I gave thee, villain?”
  9. “The pig,” quoth I, “is burn’d”: “My gold!” quoth he.
  10. “My mistress, sir,” quoth I: “Hang up thy mistress!
  11. I know not thy mistress, out on thy mistress!”
  1. Quoth who?
  1. Quoth my master.
  2. “I know,” quoth he, “no house, no wife, no mistress.”
  3. So that my arrant, due unto my tongue,
  4. I thank him, I bare home upon my shoulders:
  5. For, in conclusion, he did beat me there.
  1. Go back again, thou slave, and fetch him home.
  1. Go back again, and be new beaten home?
  2. For God’s sake send some other messenger.
  1. Back, slave, or I will break thy pate across.
  1. And he will bless that cross with other beating:
  2. Between you I shall have a holy head.
  1. Hence, prating peasant! Fetch thy master home.
  1. Am I so round with you, as you with me,
  2. That like a football you do spurn me thus?
  3. You spurn me hence, and he will spurn me hither:
  4. If I last in this service, you must case me in leather.
Exit. E. DRO.
  1. Fie, how impatience low’reth in your face!
  1. His company must do his minions grace,
  2. Whilst I at home starve for a merry look:
  3. Hath homely age th’ alluring beauty took
  4. From my poor cheek? Then he hath wasted it.
  5. Are my discourses dull? Barren my wit?
  6. If voluble and sharp discourse be marr’d,
  7. Unkindness blunts it more than marble hard.
  8. Do their gay vestments his affections bait?
  9. That’s not my fault, he’s master of my state.
  10. What ruins are in me that can be found,
  11. By him not ruin’d? Then is he the ground
  12. Of my defeatures. My decayed fair
  13. A sunny look of his would soon repair.
  14. But, too unruly deer, he breaks the pale,
  15. And feeds from home; poor I am but his stale.
  1. Self-harming jealousy—fie, beat it hence!
  1. Unfeeling fools can with such wrongs dispense:
  2. I know his eye doth homage otherwhere,
  3. Or else what lets it but he would be here?
  4. Sister, you know he promis’d me a chain;
  5. Would that alone a’ love he would detain,
  6. So he would keep fair quarter with his bed!
  7. I see the jewel best enamelled
  8. Will lose his beauty; yet the gold bides still
  9. That others touch and, often touching, will
  10. Where gold; and no man that hath a name
  11. By falsehood and corruption doth it shame.
  12. Since that my beauty cannot please his eye,
  13. I’ll weep what’s left away, and weeping die.
  1. How many fond fools serve mad jealousy?
Exeunt. ADR. LUC.

Scene 2

A public place.

Enter Antipholus Erotes of Syracuse. S. DRO.
  1. The gold I gave to Dromio is laid up
  2. Safe at the Centaur, and the heedful slave
  3. Is wand’red forth, in care to seek me out.
  4. By computation and mine host’s report,
  5. I could not speak with Dromio since at first
  6. I sent him from the mart! See, here he comes.
  7. Enter Dromio of Syracuse. S. ANT.
  8. How now, sir, is your merry humor alter’d?
  9. As you love strokes, so jest with me again.
  10. You know no Centaur? You receiv’d no gold?
  11. Your mistress sent to have me home to dinner?
  12. My house was at the Phoenix? Wast thou mad,
  13. That thus so madly thou didst answer me?
  1. What answer, sir? When spake I such a word?
  1. Even now, even here, not half an hour since.
  1. I did not see you since you sent me hence
  2. Home to the Centaur with the gold you gave me.
  1. Villain, thou didst deny the gold’s receipt,
  2. And toldst me of a mistress, and a dinner,
  3. For which I hope thou feltst I was displeas’d.
  1. I am glad to see you in this merry vein.
  2. What means this jest? I pray you, master, tell me.
  1. Yea, dost thou jeer and flout me in the teeth?
  2. Think’st thou I jest? Hold, take thou that, and that.
Beats Dromio. S. ANT. S. DRO.
  1. Hold, sir, for God’s sake! Now your jest is earnest,
  2. Upon what bargain do you give it me?
  1. Because that I familiarly sometimes
  2. Do use you for my fool, and chat with you,
  3. Your sauciness will jest upon my love,
  4. And make a common of my serious hours.
  5. When the sun shines, let foolish gnats make sport,
  6. But creep in crannies, when he hides his beams:
  7. If you will jest with me, know my aspect,
  8. And fashion your demeanor to my looks,
  9. Or I will beat this method in your sconce.
  1. Sconce call you it? So you would leave battering, I had rather have it a head. And you use these blows long, I must get a sconce for my head, and ensconce it too, or else I shall seek my wit in my shoulders. But I pray, sir, why am I beaten?
  1. Dost thou not know?
  1. Nothing, sir, but that I am beaten.
  1. Shall I tell you why?
  1. Ay, sir, and wherefore; for they say, every why hath a wherefore.
  1. Why first—for flouting me, and then wherefore—
  2. For urging it the second time to me.
  1. Was there ever any man thus beaten out of season,
  2. When in the why and the wherefore is neither rhyme nor reason?
  3. Well, sir, I thank you.
  1. Thank me, sir, for what?
  1. Marry, sir, for this something that you gave me for nothing.
  1. I’ll make you amends next, to give you nothing for something. But say, sir, is it dinner-time?
  1. No, sir, I think the meat wants that I have.
  1. In good time, sir: what’s that?
  1. Basting.
  1. Well, sir, then ’twill be dry.
  1. If it be, sir, I pray you eat none of it.
  1. Your reason?
  1. Lest it make you choleric, and purchase me another dry basting.
  1. Well, sir, learn to jest in good time—there’s a time for all things.
  1. I durst have denied that before you were so choleric.
  1. By what rule, sir?
  1. Marry, sir, by a rule as plain as the plain bald pate of Father Time himself.
  1. Let’s hear it.
  1. There’s no time for a man to recover his hair that grows bald by nature.
  1. May he not do it by fine and recovery?
  1. Yes, to pay a fine for a periwig, and recover the lost hair of another man.
  1. Why is Time such a niggard of hair, being (as it is) so plentiful an excrement?
  1. Because it is a blessing that he bestows on beasts, and what he hath scanted men in hair he hath given them in wit.
  1. Why, but there’s many a man hath more hair than wit.
  1. Not a man of those but he hath the wit to lose his hair.
  1. Why, thou didst conclude hairy men plain dealers without wit.
  1. The plainer dealer, the sooner lost; yet he loseth it in a kind of jollity.
  1. For what reason?
  1. For two—and sound ones too.
  1. Nay, not sound, I pray you.
  1. Sure ones then.
  1. Nay, not sure, in a thing falsing.
  1. Certain ones then.
  1. Name them.
  1. The one, to save the money that he spends in tiring; the other, that at dinner they should not drop in his porridge.
  1. You would all this time have prov’d there is no time for all things.
  1. Marry, and did, sir: namely, e’en no time to recover hair lost by nature.
  1. But your reason was not substantial, why there is no time to recover.
  1. Thus I mend it: Time himself is bald, and therefore, to the world’s end, will have bald followers.
  1. I knew ’twould be a bald conclusion. But soft, who wafts us yonder?
Enter Adriana and Luciana. ADR. LUC.
  1. Ay, ay, Antipholus, look strange and frown,
  2. Some other mistress hath thy sweet aspects:
  3. I am not Adriana, nor thy wife.
  4. The time was once, when thou unurg’d wouldst vow
  5. That never words were music to thine ear,
  6. That never object pleasing in thine eye,
  7. That never touch well welcome to thy hand,
  8. That never meat sweet-savor’d in thy taste,
  9. Unless I spake, or look’d, or touch’d, or carv’d to thee.
  10. How comes it now, my husband, O, how comes it,
  11. That thou art then estranged from thyself?
  12. Thyself I call it, being strange to me,
  13. That, undividable incorporate,
  14. Am better than thy dear self’s better part.
  15. Ah, do not tear away thyself from me;
  16. For know, my love, as easy mayst thou fall
  17. A drop of water in the breaking gulf,
